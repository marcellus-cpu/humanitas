Is Free Will an Illusion? What Neuroscience and Philosophy Actually Say

In 1983, a neuroscientist named Benjamin Libet published data that seemed to prove your brain decides before you do — that the feeling of making a choice is a story your mind tells after the fact. Forty years later, the debate is far more complex, the neuroscience is contested, and the philosophical question turns out to matter enormously for criminal justice, moral responsibility, and how we understand ourselves.

On a Tuesday in 1983, a subject in Benjamin Libet's neurophysiology laboratory at the University of California San Francisco sat in front of an oscilloscope clock and was asked to flick their wrist whenever they felt like it, noting the position of the clock hand at the moment they first felt the urge to move. Libet measured the brain's electrical activity simultaneously, looking for the "readiness potential" — a slow build-up of neural activity known since 1965 to precede voluntary movements.1

What he found unsettled philosophers, neuroscientists, and journalists for the next four decades. The readiness potential began approximately 550 milliseconds before the movement. The subjects reported becoming consciously aware of their intention to move approximately 200 milliseconds before the movement. This meant the brain was preparing to act roughly 350 milliseconds before the person felt they had made the decision to act. The unconscious neural preparation preceded the conscious will.

The implications seemed stark: if your brain is already committing to a movement before you consciously decide to move, then the decision — the felt sense of choosing — is not causing the movement. It is a post-hoc story the conscious mind tells about a process that was already underway. Free will, on this reading, is not just limited. It is an illusion.

This interpretation has been enormously influential in popular culture, neuroscience, and law. It has also been contested at almost every level — its methodology, its interpretation, the definition of free will it assumes, and the philosophical conclusions drawn from it. The full picture is considerably more complicated than the headlines suggest.

The question this article addresses: Not whether free will exists in some absolute metaphysical sense — that question may be unanswerable — but what the neuroscience actually shows, what the main philosophical positions are and how strong they are, and what the answer practically matters for how we treat each other.

The Libet Experiment and Its Problems

The Libet experiment has been replicated many times, and the basic finding — that the readiness potential precedes conscious awareness of intention — is robust. But its interpretation as evidence against free will depends on several assumptions that are scientifically and philosophically contested.

First problem: the measurement of conscious awareness is unreliable. Libet asked subjects to report the clock position when they first felt the urge to move. But the relationship between the felt moment of intention and the actual neural event that constitutes the intention is not straightforward. Subjects are using memory to report when they became aware of an impulse — and introspective reports of timing are notoriously inaccurate, subject to retrospective reconstruction, and vulnerable to post-hoc rationalisation. The margin of error in the conscious awareness measurements is large relative to the 350-millisecond gap Libet was measuring.2

Second problem: the readiness potential may not be what we think it is. A 2012 paper by Aaron Schurger and colleagues proposed that the readiness potential is not a preparation signal for a decision but a reflection of fluctuating neural noise — the brain's spontaneous background activity reaching a threshold that triggers both the movement and the retrospective feeling of having decided. On this interpretation, the readiness potential does not precede the decision at all; it simply reflects random neural fluctuations that tip over into action, and the conscious intention follows naturally from the same tipping event.3 If Schurger is right, Libet's experiment says nothing about free will — it describes how the brain tips from inaction into spontaneous movement, not how deliberate decisions are made.

Third problem: the experiment tests the wrong kind of action. Libet's subjects were asked to move whenever they felt like it — a paradigm explicitly designed to produce spontaneous, unmotivated, arbitrary wrist movements. This is not how most decisions work. Most decisions humans care about — what to study, whom to marry, whether to lie, how to vote — involve deliberation, competing reasons, weighing consequences, and moral consideration extended over time. The Libet experiment has nothing to say about these decisions. Drawing conclusions about the freedom of deliberated, reason-responsive choices from data on arbitrary wrist movements is a significant overextension.4

The Three Main Philosophical Positions

Position 01
Hard Incompatibilism — Free Will Does Not Exist
Minority position among professional philosophers

Hard incompatibilists hold that free will — understood as the ability to have done otherwise under exactly the same circumstances — is genuinely incompatible with the causal determinism of the physical world, and that since determinism (or indeterminism at the quantum level) is true, free will does not exist. Every action, thought, and decision is the product of prior causes — genetics, upbringing, neurochemistry, circumstance — that the agent did not choose and cannot override.

The most prominent contemporary hard incompatibilist is philosopher Derk Pereboom, who argues in Living Without Free Will (2001) that this conclusion does not lead to nihilism or despair but requires revising how we think about moral responsibility, criminal punishment, and interpersonal relationships. If no one could have done otherwise, retributive punishment — punishment aimed at making someone suffer for what they deserved — is unjustified. Consequentialist responses (deterrence, incapacitation, rehabilitation) remain justifiable, but the moral framework changes fundamentally.5

Key argument: The "four-case argument." Pereboom constructs four scenarios from remote causation to direct brain manipulation. In each case, our intuition is that the agent is not fully responsible. The final case — ordinary human action caused by prior factors — is structurally identical. If the agent is not responsible in the manipulated cases, they are not responsible in the ordinary case either.
Position 02
Compatibilism — Free Will and Determinism Are Not in Conflict
Majority position among professional philosophers

Compatibilism — the view that free will is compatible with determinism — is the majority position among professional philosophers, held by approximately 59% in the PhilPapers 2020 survey of over 1,700 philosophers.6 Compatibilists argue that the concept of free will relevant to moral responsibility and rational agency does not require the ability to have done otherwise in a deterministic sense. What matters is not whether your decision was caused — all decisions are caused — but whether it was caused in the right way.

On the most influential compatibilist account, associated with Harry Frankfurt and P.F. Strawson, what matters is whether the action flowed from the agent's own desires, values, and reflective endorsement — whether the person acted in accordance with who they are, responsively to reasons, free from compulsion, manipulation, or neurological impairment.7 A kleptomaniac who steals compulsively, despite wanting not to, lacks free will in the relevant sense. A person who deliberates, weighs reasons, and acts on their considered judgement has free will in the relevant sense — even if, in some deep physical sense, their brain's activity was determined by prior causes.

Key insight: The question "could you have done otherwise?" is ambiguous. Under physical determinism, no — you could not have done otherwise given exactly the same prior conditions. But in the sense relevant to agency — could you have done otherwise if you had wanted to, reasoned differently, or had different values? — yes. Compatibilism says the second sense is what matters for responsibility.
Position 03
Libertarianism (Philosophical) — Free Will Exists and Requires Indeterminism
Minority position, contested by determinism

Philosophical libertarians (note: this has nothing to do with political libertarianism) hold that free will is genuinely incompatible with determinism, and that therefore genuine free will requires some form of indeterminism — events not fully determined by prior causes. Some versions appeal to quantum indeterminacy as a source of undetermined choices; others appeal to a special causal power of rational agents that cannot be reduced to prior physical causes.

The main challenge for libertarianism is explaining how random (indeterminate) events in the brain constitute free choices. If quantum randomness introduces genuine indeterminacy, it is not clear that random choices are any more "free" or attributable to the agent than determined ones — a random neurological event causing an action is not obviously different from a determined one in terms of the agent's responsibility for it. Libertarian accounts that appeal to a special kind of agent causation face the challenge of explaining what that causation is and how it relates to physical processes without violating physical laws.8

Key tension: If determinism removes free will because your choices were fixed in advance, does quantum randomness restore it? Not obviously — "your neurons fired randomly" is not the same as "you chose freely."

Two Full Case Studies: Why the Answer Actually Matters

Case Study 01 — Free Will and Criminal Justice

The Neuroscience Defence in Court and Its Limits

The practical implications of free will philosophy are nowhere more consequential than in criminal law, which traditionally rests on the assumption that people could have chosen otherwise — that punishment is a response to a free choice to do wrong. If that assumption is undermined by neuroscience, the moral foundations of punishment change dramatically.

In the United States, neuroscience evidence has increasingly been introduced in criminal cases, particularly in sentencing hearings. A 2012 review by Nita Farahany found that neuroscience evidence appeared in over 1,500 judicial opinions from 2005 to 2012, predominantly in criminal cases.9 The most common uses: brain imaging evidence of reduced frontal lobe activity or abnormal structure in defendants who committed violent crimes, introduced to argue for mitigated culpability. The landmark 2005 US Supreme Court case Roper v. Simmons held that executing juvenile offenders was unconstitutional, partly on the basis of evidence that the adolescent brain is neurologically different in ways affecting impulse control and future orientation.10

But the neuroscience defence runs into a serious philosophical problem that courts are still grappling with: if every criminal action is the product of a brain that was shaped by causes the defendant did not choose (genetics, trauma, poverty, neurological difference), then every criminal action could in principle be excused by neuroscience. As philosopher Stephen Morse has argued, neuroscientific evidence of brain differences is legally relevant only if it establishes that the defendant lacked the capacity for rational agency — not merely that their behaviour was caused by their brain.11 Every human behaviour is caused by a brain. Causation by a brain is not the same as the absence of agency.

The most defensible position draws on compatibilism: criminal responsibility does not require that a defendant could have done otherwise in an absolute metaphysical sense, but that they had the capacity to respond to reasons, were not acting under compulsion or severe impairment, and understood what they were doing. Severe neurological impairment that genuinely undermines reason-responsiveness is relevant. Normal neural variation that reflects a person's character and history is not, on this view, an excuse.

Key lesson: Neuroscience can inform the question of criminal responsibility but cannot resolve it. The relevant question is capacity for rational agency, not the physical causes of behaviour. Compatibilist frameworks are more useful to law than hard incompatibilist ones.
Case Study 02 — The Psychological Consequences of Disbelieving in Free Will

What Happens When People Are Told Free Will Is Illusory

Philosophers Kathleen Vohs and Jonathan Schooler conducted a revealing series of experiments asking what happens to human behaviour when people are induced to disbelieve in free will. Subjects who read passages arguing that free will is an illusion subsequently showed measurably reduced prosocial behaviour: they cheated more on mathematics tests, were less likely to help others who "accidentally" dropped items, and showed reduced motivation to engage in effortful tasks.12

Subsequent research confirmed and extended these findings. A cross-cultural study by Baumeister, Masicampo, and DeWall found that induced disbelief in free will was associated with increased aggression and reduced cooperation across multiple cultural contexts. A longitudinal study in an occupational setting found that employees who scored lower on a belief in free will were rated as performing worse by supervisors and showed higher rates of unethical behaviour.13

These findings do not prove that free will exists. They demonstrate that belief in free will has functional consequences for behaviour — that people who believe their choices matter, behave better than people who believe they do not. This raises a distinctive practical question that goes beyond the metaphysical one: even if hard incompatibilism is philosophically correct, does communicating that conclusion have costs that argue for discretion in how, and to whom, it is communicated?

The philosopher and neuroscientist Sam Harris, one of the most prominent popular advocates of the "free will is an illusion" position, acknowledges this concern but argues that the short-term behavioural effects of disbelief are outweighed by the longer-term benefits of accurate understanding — particularly for reducing harsh retributive attitudes toward criminals and increasing compassion.14 This empirical claim remains contested.

Key lesson: Whether or not free will exists, believing in it has measurable effects on human behaviour — particularly prosocial behaviour, honesty, and effort. This creates a distinctive ethical dimension to the public communication of free will research.

What Are the Practical Consequences of Each Position?

If you accept each position, here is what follows

Hard Incompatibilism

Retributive punishment (punishing because deserved) is unjustified. Criminal justice should focus on deterrence, incapacitation, and rehabilitation. Anger and resentment toward wrongdoers are understandable but philosophically unwarranted. Greater compassion for those shaped by difficult circumstances follows naturally.

Compatibilism

Moral responsibility is real when actions flow from a person's own reasons and values free from compulsion. Punishment for reason-responsive choices is justified. Compassion for those whose capacity for rational agency was genuinely impaired is also justified. Most of ordinary moral and legal practice survives intact.

Libertarianism

Full moral responsibility for freely chosen actions. Retributive punishment is fully justified. The challenge is specifying what the indeterminate causation is and why quantum randomness or agent causation constitutes freedom rather than mere chance.

Practical takeaway

Whatever position is correct, the empirical evidence that believing in free will improves behaviour suggests caution about aggressive popular communication of hard incompatibilism. The philosophical question and the question of what to teach are not the same question.

Where the Debate Currently Stands

The free will debate is genuinely unresolved — not because philosophers are confused, but because it involves questions that are extraordinarily difficult to answer: the relationship between physical causation and rational agency, the nature of consciousness and its causal role, and what "could have done otherwise" actually means. The 2020 PhilPapers survey found that professional philosophers favoured compatibilism (59%) over hard determinism / no free will (11%) and libertarianism (11%), with the remainder accepting other positions or none.15

What is clear from the best current work is that the Libet experiment does not settle the question — its methodology is too limited and its interpretation too contested to bear the philosophical weight that has been placed upon it. What is also clear is that the question matters: the answer has direct implications for criminal justice, moral education, interpersonal relationships, and how we understand ourselves.

"Before worrying about whether you have free will, you should ask what kind of free will you want and whether it is coherent. Most of the 'free will is an illusion' literature is responding to a concept of free will that serious philosophers of mind do not actually endorse."

— Daniel Dennett, Professor of Philosophy, Tufts University, Freedom Evolves (2003), p. 1416

The most sophisticated current view among philosophers of mind holds that free will is best understood not as a metaphysical exemption from causation but as a particular kind of causal capacity — the capacity to be moved by reasons, to deliberate, to act in accordance with values one reflectively endorses, and to be responsive to the evidence and arguments one encounters. That capacity is real, is partly what distinguishes humans from simple automatons, and is worth protecting and cultivating — regardless of how the deeper metaphysical questions are ultimately resolved.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does quantum mechanics give us free will?
Quantum indeterminacy — the genuine randomness at the quantum level described by quantum mechanics — is sometimes proposed as a physical basis for free will, the idea being that if events are not fully determined by prior causes, then choices could be genuinely undetermined. The problem is that quantum randomness does not obviously help with free will. Random is not the same as free. If a quantum event in your brain randomly fires a neuron that causes you to punch someone, it is not clear that the randomness makes the action more "yours" or more something you should be held responsible for. Most philosophers who care about free will want choices to be attributable to the agent — to be expressions of the agent's character, values, and reasons — and random quantum events do not satisfy this requirement any better than deterministic causes. The more promising question is not whether quantum mechanics restores free will but whether the concept of free will we should care about requires anything beyond what compatibilism provides.
If we don't have free will, why do we experience ourselves as choosing?
Compatibilists and hard incompatibilists agree that the experience of deliberation and choice is real — nobody is denying that you genuinely deliberate, weigh options, and feel yourself making decisions. The question is what that experience consists in and what causal role it plays. For compatibilists, the experience of choosing just is the process of rational deliberation — being moved by reasons, evaluating options against values, and acting accordingly. This is a genuine causal process even if it is a physical one. For hard incompatibilists, the experience is real but doesn't constitute the kind of free choice that would ground retributive moral responsibility. The experience of choosing is not in dispute; its metaphysical significance is.
Can neuroscience ever prove or disprove free will?
Almost certainly not on its own. The problem is that "free will" is a philosophical concept, not a physical one — and neuroscience can only measure physical processes. You could show, as the Libet experiment does, that neural preparation precedes conscious awareness. You could show, as the Soon et al. 2008 fMRI study does, that predictive activity exists up to 10 seconds before a reported decision. None of this settles the philosophical question because the philosophical question is about what freedom and responsibility require, not about what physical processes are present. Compatibilists will say that showing brain activity precedes conscious awareness doesn't undermine agency if the overall deliberative process that led to the decision was still the right kind. Hard incompatibilists will say it supports their view. The neuroscience is evidence that bears on the free will debate but cannot resolve it without a philosophical framework that specifies what would count as evidence for or against free will.
What does compatibilism mean practically for how I think about my own choices?
If compatibilism is correct — which represents the majority view among professional philosophers — then the fact that your choices are caused by your character, history, brain, and circumstances does not undermine them. Your choices are genuinely yours precisely because they flow from your values, reasons, and deliberations. The person who deliberates carefully, considers consequences, reflects on their values, and acts accordingly is exercising free will in the sense that matters — even though all of this is a physical process in a physical brain. Practically, this means your choices matter, your efforts at self-improvement matter, and your responsibility for your actions is real. The philosophical complexity does not dissolve into paralysis or nihilism — it clarifies what freedom actually means: acting from your own reasons, rather than from compulsion, manipulation, or impaired capacity.
