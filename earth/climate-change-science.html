Climate Change — The Complete Science Explained

The physics of the greenhouse effect has been understood since 1859. The evidence that human activity is driving global warming is now so overwhelming that it is endorsed by every major scientific institution on Earth. This is a complete, accurate account of the science — how it works, what we know with certainty, what remains uncertain, and what the evidence says about what is still possible.

In 1859, an Irish physicist named John Tyndall conducted a series of experiments measuring how different gases absorbed and re-emitted radiant heat. He found that water vapour and carbon dioxide — trace gases in the atmosphere — were remarkably effective at trapping infrared radiation, while nitrogen and oxygen — which make up 99% of the atmosphere — were almost completely transparent to it. Tyndall immediately understood the implication: the atmosphere functions as a thermal blanket, and changing its composition would change the temperature of the Earth. He published his findings that year. The physics of the greenhouse effect has been understood for over 165 years.1

In 2023, the global mean surface temperature was 1.45°C above the pre-industrial average — the highest annual anomaly ever recorded. Every one of the ten hottest years on record has occurred since 2010. The concentration of CO₂ in the atmosphere reached 421 parts per million in 2024 — a level not seen on Earth for at least 3 million years, before our species existed.2

The science of climate change is not contested among scientists. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change's Sixth Assessment Report, published 2021–2023 and drawing on thousands of peer-reviewed studies, concluded that it is "unequivocal that human influence has warmed the atmosphere, ocean and land." This article explains the science behind that conclusion, what is already happening, and what the evidence says about what is still possible.

What this article covers: The physics of the greenhouse effect, the evidence for human causation, current observed impacts, projected future scenarios, and what the science says about solutions. It does not cover climate politics or policy in detail — those are important but separate from the underlying science, which is what this article addresses.

The Physics — How the Greenhouse Effect Works

The Earth's temperature is determined by a balance between incoming solar radiation and outgoing infrared radiation. The Sun emits energy primarily as visible light and ultraviolet radiation, which passes relatively freely through the atmosphere. The Earth absorbs this energy and re-emits it as infrared (heat) radiation. Greenhouse gases — including water vapour, CO₂, methane, nitrous oxide, and ozone — absorb this outgoing infrared radiation and re-emit it in all directions, including back toward Earth's surface. This is the greenhouse effect, and without it, Earth's average temperature would be approximately −18°C rather than the +15°C that supports life.3

The problem is not the greenhouse effect itself — it is essential — but the enhancement of it by human emissions. Since the Industrial Revolution, the burning of fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas) has added approximately 2.4 trillion tonnes of CO₂ to the atmosphere. Deforestation — which destroys the carbon sinks that absorb atmospheric CO₂ — has added further. Methane emissions from agriculture, landfills, and fossil fuel extraction have also increased significantly. The result is an enhanced greenhouse effect that traps more heat than the pre-industrial system, raising global temperatures.4

Five Lines of Evidence for Human Causation

1 — Isotopic Fingerprinting
Strongest direct evidence

Carbon has three isotopes: C-12, C-13, and C-14. Fossil fuels are depleted in C-13 and contain essentially no C-14 (which decays over thousands of years). As fossil fuel CO₂ is added to the atmosphere, the ratio of C-13 and C-14 in atmospheric CO₂ measurably decreases — a chemical fingerprint that points specifically to fossil fuel burning as the source of the increase, as opposed to natural sources like volcanic emissions or ocean outgassing.5

This is not a model or projection — it is a direct chemical measurement. The atmospheric isotope ratio has been shifting in exactly the direction expected from fossil fuel combustion since measurements began, at exactly the rate predicted from known emissions volumes.
2 — Stratospheric Cooling
Counter-intuitive but decisive

If warming were caused by the Sun outputting more energy, the entire atmosphere — including the stratosphere — would warm. But satellite measurements show that while the troposphere (lower atmosphere) has been warming, the stratosphere has been cooling. This is exactly what greenhouse gas theory predicts: greenhouse gases trap heat in the lower atmosphere, meaning less heat reaches the stratosphere. Solar variability, by contrast, would warm both layers simultaneously.6

The stratospheric cooling signature is one of the most decisive pieces of evidence for greenhouse gas forcing versus solar forcing — it is a discriminating test that clearly distinguishes between the two hypotheses.
3 — Ocean Heat Content
The planet's thermometer

The oceans have absorbed approximately 90% of the excess heat trapped by enhanced greenhouse gases since 1971. Ocean heat content — measured by thousands of Argo floats deployed globally — has been rising consistently for decades. The oceans are not just warming at the surface but throughout their depth, which requires sustained, large-scale energy input rather than any short-term variability. The pattern of ocean warming matches climate model predictions for greenhouse gas forcing.7

4 — Night Warming Faster Than Day
Greenhouse signature confirmed

Greenhouse gases trap outgoing infrared radiation, which the Earth emits continuously — but more of which is emitted at night when there is no competing solar input. Greenhouse warming theory therefore predicts that nights should warm faster than days. Temperature records confirm this: minimum temperatures (nights) have been rising approximately twice as fast as maximum temperatures (days). Solar-driven warming would produce the opposite pattern.8

5 — Palaeoclimate Correlation
800,000 years of evidence

Ice cores from Antarctica provide atmospheric records going back 800,000 years, capturing ancient air bubbles with known CO₂ concentrations alongside isotopic temperature proxies. These records show a strong correlation between CO₂ concentrations and temperature throughout eight glacial cycles — when CO₂ was high, temperatures were high; when CO₂ was low, temperatures were low. Current CO₂ levels of 421ppm are far outside the range of natural variation over this entire 800,000-year record (which ranged between approximately 180–280ppm).9

What Is Already Happening — Current Observed Impacts

Extreme Heat Events

Heat waves that would have occurred once per 50 years in the pre-industrial climate now occur approximately 5 times more frequently. The probability of any given heat extreme has increased sharply — the 2021 Pacific Northwest heat dome, which reached 49.6°C in Canada and killed over 600 people, was made at least 150 times more likely by climate change.

Sea Level Rise

Global mean sea level has risen approximately 20cm since 1900, with the rate accelerating — from 1.3mm/year in the early 20th century to 3.7mm/year in the 2010s. The primary drivers are thermal expansion of warming oceans and ice sheet melt. Low-lying regions and island nations face existential risk at continued warming.

Ocean Acidification

The oceans have absorbed approximately 30% of human CO₂ emissions, converting it to carbonic acid. Ocean pH has dropped by 0.1 units since the industrial revolution — a 26% increase in acidity. This disrupts calcium carbonate formation, threatening coral reefs, shellfish, and the base of marine food webs.

Arctic Amplification

The Arctic is warming approximately 4 times faster than the global average — a phenomenon called Arctic amplification. Arctic sea ice extent in summer 2023 was the lowest ever recorded. Permafrost thaw is releasing stored methane and CO₂, potentially triggering feedback loops that accelerate warming beyond human control.

Precipitation Extremes

A warmer atmosphere holds more water vapour (approximately 7% more per degree of warming). This intensifies both heavy precipitation events and droughts — wet regions tend to get wetter and dry regions drier. The frequency of very heavy rainfall events has increased across most of the world.

Ecosystem Disruption

Species ranges are shifting poleward and to higher elevations. Phenological timing — the seasonal cues for migration, flowering, and breeding — is disrupting evolved relationships between species. Coral bleaching events have increased in frequency and severity. Approximately one million species face heightened extinction risk from climate-driven habitat loss.

Two Case Studies: Climate Change in East Africa

Case Study 01 — The East African Food Security Crisis

How Climate Change Is Reshaping Agriculture Across the Horn of Africa

Between 2020 and 2023, East Africa experienced its worst drought in 40 years — five consecutive failed rainy seasons affecting Ethiopia, Kenya, Somalia, and Djibouti. Over 36 million people faced severe food insecurity. Livestock mortality in pastoral communities reached catastrophic levels in some areas, with herders reporting losing 50–80% of their animals. The UN World Food Programme described it as the most severe humanitarian crisis the region had seen in a generation.

Climate attribution science — the field that quantifies the contribution of climate change to specific weather events — found that human-caused climate change made the drought significantly more likely and more intense. The failure of the East African long rains has been linked to warming of the Indian Ocean, which alters the moisture dynamics that drive East African rainfall patterns. A 2023 study published in Nature Climate Change found that the probability of five consecutive failed rainy seasons in the region had increased by more than 100 times due to climate change.

Kenya's own climate vulnerability is acute. The country depends heavily on rain-fed agriculture, with approximately 80% of its land classified as arid or semi-arid. Rainfall variability is already the primary driver of economic volatility — a bad rain year contracts Kenya's GDP measurably. As the climate continues to warm, the frequency of drought years is projected to increase significantly, with major implications for food security, pastoral livelihoods, and economic stability.10

Key lesson: Climate change is not a future threat for East Africa — it is a present one, already reshaping agriculture, water security, and humanitarian conditions across the region.
Case Study 02 — The Melting of Mount Kenya

Africa's Second-Highest Peak Is Losing Its Ice — and What That Means

Mount Kenya's glaciers have shrunk by approximately 90% since measurements began in the late 19th century. The Lewis Glacier — the largest on the mountain and a subject of continuous scientific monitoring — has retreated by over 350 metres since 1934. At current rates of retreat, all glaciers on Mount Kenya are projected to disappear entirely within 20–30 years.

The ecological and hydrological consequences extend far beyond the mountain itself. Mount Kenya's glaciers and permanent snowfields feed the Ewaso Ng'iro and Tana rivers, which are critical water sources for millions of people and for Kenya's agricultural sector. As glacier volume declines, dry-season river flows are already decreasing. Communities downstream that have relied on this water for generations are facing increasing water stress in the dry season, even as total annual precipitation remains relatively stable.

The loss of Mount Kenya's glaciers is also culturally and symbolically significant. The mountain is sacred to the Kikuyu people, for whom it is the dwelling place of God (Ngai) and the orientation point of their prayers and homes. The disappearance of its distinctive ice cap is not merely an environmental statistic but the transformation of a living cultural and spiritual landmark.

Key lesson: Glacial retreat in East Africa is not an abstract statistic — it directly threatens water security for millions of people and represents an irreversible loss of natural and cultural heritage.

What the Science Says About Solutions

The IPCC's Sixth Assessment Report is explicit: limiting warming to 1.5°C requires halving global emissions by 2030 and reaching net zero by 2050. This is technically feasible — the required technologies exist — but requires transformational changes to the energy system, land use, and economy at a pace that has no precedent in human history.

The key findings from the solutions science are the following. Renewable energy is now the cheapest form of new electricity generation in most of the world — solar and wind costs have fallen by more than 90% since 2010, making the clean energy transition economically attractive as well as environmentally necessary. Energy efficiency improvements in buildings, transport, and industry can reduce emissions substantially at negative or minimal net cost. Protecting and restoring forests and other carbon sinks is essential — deforestation releases CO₂ while reforestation sequesters it. Carbon capture technologies exist but are currently expensive and limited in scale. Methane emissions — which have a much higher short-term warming impact than CO₂ — can be reduced rapidly through changes to agricultural and fossil fuel practices.11

The critical variable is not technology but political will and policy design. Every year of delay makes the task harder and the costs higher — both the costs of transition and the costs of the physical impacts of additional warming that will occur in the meantime.

"The scientific evidence is unequivocal: climate change is a threat to human wellbeing and the health of the planet. Any further delay in concerted global action will miss a brief and rapidly closing window to secure a liveable future."

— IPCC Sixth Assessment Report, Working Group II Summary for Policymakers (2022), p. 3412

Frequently Asked Questions

Has the climate changed naturally before? Doesn't that mean current change is natural too?
The climate has changed naturally throughout Earth's history — ice ages, warm periods, and mass extinction events have all involved significant climate shifts. But this does not mean current warming is natural. The critical questions are: what is causing the change, how fast is it happening, and is the pattern consistent with known natural drivers or with greenhouse gas forcing? The answers are clear: current warming is occurring at 10–100 times the rate of natural climate shifts seen in the geological record; it matches the chemical signature (isotopic composition) of fossil fuel combustion rather than natural CO₂ sources; and it is occurring simultaneously with measurable increases in atmospheric CO₂ driven by known human emissions. The existence of past natural climate change does not negate evidence of present human-caused change, any more than the existence of natural forest fires negates evidence of arson in a specific fire.
Is it too late to do anything about climate change?
No — but the window for limiting warming to manageable levels is narrowing rapidly. Every tenth of a degree of avoided warming meaningfully reduces impacts. The difference between 1.5°C and 2°C of warming is not minor — at 2°C, coral reef loss exceeds 99% versus 70–90% at 1.5°C; hundreds of millions more people face extreme heat; sea level rise projections increase significantly. Limiting warming to 1.5°C is now extremely difficult but technically possible if emissions are halved by 2030 and reach net zero by 2050. Limiting warming to 2°C is more achievable. Every tonne of CO₂ not emitted makes a measurable difference to outcomes — the science is unambiguous that action now reduces future harm, even if some warming is already locked in by past emissions.
What is the difference between climate change and global warming?
Global warming refers specifically to the rise in average global surface temperatures driven by greenhouse gas emissions. Climate change is the broader term that encompasses all the changes to Earth's climate system driven by that warming — including changes in precipitation patterns, sea level rise, ocean acidification, changes in storm intensity and frequency, ecosystem disruption, and shifts in seasonal timing. "Climate change" is the more accurate and comprehensive term for what is happening, which is why it is now preferred by scientists over "global warming." The two terms are sometimes used interchangeably, but climate change captures the full range of effects rather than just the temperature component.
How does climate change affect Africa specifically?
Africa is particularly vulnerable to climate change for several interconnected reasons. First, it is disproportionately exposed to the impacts — higher baseline temperatures mean that warming pushes more African regions above human tolerance thresholds; rainfall variability, already high, is projected to increase; and coastal regions face sea level rise. Second, African economies are disproportionately dependent on climate-sensitive sectors — rain-fed agriculture, pastoralism, fisheries, and water resources. Third, adaptive capacity is constrained by economic development levels — wealthy countries can build sea walls, air-condition buildings, and import food when harvests fail; many African countries cannot. The cruel irony is that Africa has contributed the least to cumulative historical emissions while facing among the greatest climate impacts. This equity dimension is central to international climate negotiations and to the concept of climate finance from wealthy to developing nations.
