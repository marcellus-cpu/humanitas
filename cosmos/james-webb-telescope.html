What the James Webb Space Telescope Has Discovered — and Why It Is Rewriting Cosmology

Launched on Christmas Day 2021, the James Webb Space Telescope has already produced images of the universe's first galaxies, detected carbon dioxide in exoplanet atmospheres, and found galaxies so massive and so early that they should not exist according to standard cosmological models. This is what it has found — and what it means.

On Christmas Day 2021, an Ariane 5 rocket lifted off from the Guiana Space Centre carrying the most complex scientific instrument ever built by human hands. The James Webb Space Telescope — 25 years in development, delayed 14 times, and costing $10 billion — began a month-long journey to its operational position 1.5 million kilometres from Earth.1 Scientists and engineers held their breath through 344 individual deployment steps, any one of which could have ended the mission. Every one succeeded. On July 11, 2022, NASA released the first full-colour scientific images. They were unlike anything that had been seen before.

Webb is not simply a better telescope than its predecessors. It operates in a fundamentally different way, observing the universe primarily in infrared light rather than visible light. This distinction is decisive for cosmology: as the universe expands, light from the most distant objects is stretched — redshifted — from its original wavelengths into the infrared. The oldest light in the universe, from the first galaxies formed hundreds of millions of years after the Big Bang, arrives at Earth as infrared radiation invisible to optical telescopes but detectable by Webb's instruments. Webb can see further back in time than any previous telescope, and what it has found is already challenging some of cosmology's most established models.

How to read cosmic distance: Astronomers use redshift (denoted z) to measure both distance and look-back time. A galaxy at z = 1 is seen as it was roughly 7.7 billion years ago — about halfway through cosmic time. A galaxy at z = 10 is seen as it was approximately 13.2 billion years ago, just 500 million years after the Big Bang. Webb has confirmed galaxies at z = 13–16, observing the universe as it existed 200–300 million years after the Big Bang — a period previously largely inaccessible to observation.

What Makes Webb Different: The Technical Foundation of the Discoveries

Understanding Webb's discoveries requires understanding what makes it unique. The key parameters are its mirror size, wavelength sensitivity, and operating temperature.

Mirror area4.5 m²25.4 m² — 6.25× larger

The operating temperature is critical and explains why Webb required such an elaborate sunshield — a five-layer membrane the size of a tennis court that keeps its instruments permanently in shadow. Infrared detectors must be cooler than the radiation they are trying to detect, or the instrument's own heat overwhelms the signal. At −233°C, Webb's MIRI instrument can detect the faint infrared glow of objects whose light has been travelling for over 13 billion years.2

The Seven Major Discoveries: What Webb Has Found and What It Means

Discovery 01
Galaxies That Should Not Exist — The Early Universe Problem
2022–2023

Within months of beginning science operations, Webb detected galaxies at redshifts between z = 10 and z = 16 that were far more massive and luminous than standard cosmological models predicted should exist at such early times. The standard model of galaxy formation — the Lambda-CDM (cold dark matter) model — holds that galaxies grow gradually by accumulating matter over billions of years, with the largest galaxies forming relatively late in cosmic history.

Webb found galaxies from when the universe was only 300–500 million years old that were already as massive as the Milky Way — galaxies that should have taken billions of years to build appearing fully formed within the first few hundred million years of cosmic time. A 2023 analysis by Labbé and colleagues identified six candidate galaxies from the first 700 million years of the universe with stellar masses comparable to or exceeding our own galaxy — masses that exceeded theoretical predictions by factors of ten or more.3

The implication: Either the standard Lambda-CDM model requires significant revision, or these galaxies formed through processes not currently accounted for — perhaps bursts of star formation far more intense than models predict, or different initial conditions in the early universe. This is one of the most significant cosmological puzzles Webb has opened.
Discovery 02
Carbon Dioxide in an Exoplanet Atmosphere — First Definitive Detection
2022

In August 2022, Webb made the first definitive detection of carbon dioxide (CO₂) in the atmosphere of an exoplanet — WASP-39b, a giant gas planet about 700 light-years from Earth. The detection was made using transmission spectroscopy: as the planet passed in front of its star, starlight filtered through the planet's atmosphere, and different molecules absorbed different wavelengths. Webb's NIRISS and NIRSpec instruments identified the characteristic absorption signature of CO₂ at 4.3 micrometres with unmistakable clarity.4

This matters not because carbon dioxide in a hot gas giant is particularly surprising, but because it demonstrated Webb's capability to detect atmospheric molecules in exoplanets with a precision that opens the door to searching for biosignatures — molecules like methane, ozone, and water vapour in the atmospheres of potentially habitable rocky planets. WASP-39b was a proof of concept; the scientific target is Earth-sized planets in habitable zones.

The implication: Webb has demonstrated the instrumental capability to detect atmospheric chemistry in other worlds. Whether it will find signs of life depends on whether it observes the right planets — and the right atmospheric combinations. Analysis of biosignature candidates will require years of observation time on specific target systems.
Discovery 03
TRAPPIST-1b — First Rocky Exoplanet Atmosphere Search
2023

The TRAPPIST-1 system — seven rocky planets orbiting an ultra-cool dwarf star 40 light-years away, three of which are in the habitable zone — has been a priority target since its discovery in 2017. In 2023, Webb observed TRAPPIST-1b (the innermost planet) using thermal emission — measuring the infrared heat coming from the planet's dayside — and found that the planet appears to lack a thick atmosphere. The measured temperature (approximately 230°C) is consistent with a bare rock with no significant atmosphere to redistribute heat.5

This is a significant finding even as a negative result. It demonstrates that Webb can characterise rocky exoplanet atmospheres (or their absence) and sets the stage for observations of the more promising TRAPPIST-1e, f, and g — the habitable zone planets — in future observation cycles. The search for signs of life in the TRAPPIST system will be one of Webb's most significant long-term scientific programmes.

Discovery 04
The Most Distant Confirmed Galaxy — JADES-GS-z14-0
2024

In May 2024, astronomers using Webb's JADES (JWST Advanced Deep Extragalactic Survey) programme announced the confirmation of JADES-GS-z14-0 — the most distant galaxy ever spectroscopically confirmed, at a redshift of z = 14.32. This galaxy existed when the universe was only approximately 290 million years old — less than 2% of its current age.6

What makes this discovery particularly striking is not just the distance but the galaxy's properties. JADES-GS-z14-0 is large and bright for its era — it contains hundreds of millions of solar masses in stars and appears to be actively forming new stars at a high rate. The presence of oxygen emission lines in its spectrum indicates that at least one generation of stars had already formed and died, enriching the galaxy with heavier elements, within the first 300 million years of the universe. This is earlier than stellar evolution models suggested was possible.

The implication: Star formation and chemical enrichment in the early universe proceeded much faster than current models account for. The first stars must have formed within the first 100–200 million years after the Big Bang — possibly even earlier.
Discovery 05
Water, Carbon Dioxide, and Organic Molecules in a Star-Forming Region
2023

Webb's MIRI instrument observed the Chameleon I molecular cloud — a stellar nursery approximately 630 light-years from Earth — and detected an unexpectedly rich inventory of molecular ices in the cold dust surrounding young protostars. The detections included water ice, carbon dioxide, ammonia, methanol, and complex organic molecules including methyl cyanide (CH₃CN) and dimethyl ether.7

This is directly relevant to the question of how life's building blocks are distributed in the universe. The organic molecules detected in Chameleon I are the same classes of compounds found in comets, meteorites, and the interstellar medium — and they are the precursors from which amino acids and other biological molecules can form. Their presence in a star-forming region indicates that the chemical inventory needed for prebiotic chemistry is routinely assembled before planets even form.

Discovery 06
Gravitational Lensing Arcs and Einstein Rings in Unprecedented Detail
2022–2024

General relativity predicts that massive objects curve spacetime, bending light around them — an effect called gravitational lensing. When a massive galaxy cluster lies between Earth and a more distant galaxy, the cluster can act as a natural cosmic lens, magnifying and distorting the distant galaxy into arcs or complete rings (Einstein rings). Hubble had observed this effect, but Webb's infrared sensitivity and sharper resolution have produced lensed images of astonishing detail.

Webb's observations of galaxy clusters including SMACS 0723, Abell 2744, and El Gordo have produced the most detailed views of gravitationally lensed objects ever obtained, revealing internal structure within distant galaxies visible only because the lensing magnification can reach factors of 50–100×. These observations allow astronomers to study galaxies that would otherwise be invisible in extraordinary detail.8

The implication: Galaxy clusters function as free, natural telescopes. Webb's combination with gravitational lensing creates an effective aperture far beyond what any human-built telescope could achieve. Some of the most detailed observations of early universe galaxies will come from this combination.
Discovery 07
Direct Imaging of Exoplanets — HIP 65426b
2022

In September 2022, Webb captured direct images of an exoplanet — HIP 65426b, a young giant planet about 6–12 times the mass of Jupiter, 385 light-years away — in multiple infrared wavelength bands simultaneously. Direct imaging of exoplanets is extraordinarily difficult because planets are typically billions of times fainter than their host stars; in Webb's images, the planet appears as a distinct point of light separate from its star.9

This was Webb's first direct exoplanet image and demonstrated capabilities critical for future atmospheric characterisation. Directly imaged planets can be studied in far greater detail than planets detected by transit (crossing their star's face) or radial velocity (star wobble) methods. The long-term programme includes imaging young planetary systems still in formation and comparing their properties to models of how our own Solar System evolved.

Two Full Case Studies: When Webb Surprised Everyone

Case Study 01 — The Hubble Tension and Webb's Role

A Crack in Cosmology's Foundation — Is the Universe Expanding Faster Than We Thought?

The "Hubble tension" refers to a persistent discrepancy in measurements of the Hubble constant — the rate at which the universe is currently expanding. Measurements from the Cosmic Microwave Background (the afterglow of the Big Bang, observed by the Planck satellite) give a value of approximately 67.4 km/s/Mpc. But measurements of the expansion rate using "distance ladder" methods — observing Cepheid variable stars and Type Ia supernovae in nearby galaxies — consistently give a higher value, approximately 73 km/s/Mpc.

The discrepancy is approximately 9%, and it has grown more statistically significant as measurements have improved. If the measurements are correct on both sides, it means standard cosmology is missing something fundamental — either about the nature of dark energy, the nature of dark matter, or physics in the very early universe.

Webb has contributed to this debate in a specific way. One hypothesis was that the distance ladder measurements were systematically biased because Hubble's resolution was insufficient to cleanly separate Cepheid variable stars from nearby stars in crowded galaxy regions — an effect called "crowding bias." Webb's sharper infrared resolution should be able to resolve this. A 2023 analysis by Freedman and colleagues using Webb observations found that crowding bias was real but smaller than hoped — it did not resolve the tension. A separate 2023 analysis by Riess and the SH0ES team confirmed their Hubble-based results using Webb data, suggesting the higher expansion rate is genuine.10

As of 2025, the Hubble tension remains unresolved and is considered one of the most significant open problems in cosmology. Webb has not solved it — but it has definitively established that it is real.

Key finding: The Hubble tension is a genuine discrepancy, not a measurement artefact. Webb has ruled out the most plausible systematic error explanation, increasing the likelihood that new physics is required to resolve it.
Case Study 02 — Stephan's Quintet and the Power of Multi-Wavelength Observation

A 40-Year-Old Image Revisited — What Webb Revealed That Hubble Could Not

Stephan's Quintet — a group of five galaxies in the constellation Pegasus, four of which are in gravitational interaction with each other — has been photographed by telescopes for over a century and was one of the first targets of Hubble's Wide Field Camera 3. Webb's mosaic image of the group, released with its first science images in July 2022, covers approximately 150 million light-years and consists of nearly 1,000 individual image files composited together — the largest Webb image released to that date.

What Webb revealed that Hubble could not: in the region where one of the five galaxies (NGC 7318b) is plowing through the group at roughly 800 km/s, creating a massive shockwave, Webb's MIRI instrument detected molecular hydrogen emission and carbon monoxide in the shock front — evidence of gas being heated, compressed, and chemically transformed in real-time by the collision. This kind of interacting molecular chemistry in galaxy collisions had been theorised but not directly observed before Webb.11

The image itself became culturally significant: with resolution at 5,000 × 2,400 pixels and multiple wavelength bands colour-composed, it remains one of the most widely reproduced astronomical images ever produced. But the science behind the aesthetic is as significant as the image — it demonstrates Webb's capacity to study galaxy interactions and the physics of intergalactic shocks in ways previously inaccessible.

Key finding: Webb's multi-instrument approach — seeing simultaneously in near-infrared and mid-infrared — reveals physical processes in galactic interactions that single-wavelength observations entirely miss. Stephan's Quintet demonstrates the power of this combined observational strategy.

What Comes Next: Webb's Remaining Science Programme

Webb's operational lifetime is estimated at 20 years or more — its launch was precise enough to conserve propellant far beyond the design requirement of 10 years. The science programme is substantial and ongoing. Among the highest-priority future observations:

  • TRAPPIST-1 habitable zone planets: Extended observations of TRAPPIST-1e, 1f, and 1g over multiple cycles to search for atmospheric signatures and potential biosignatures. This may be the most significant search for signs of life in the history of astronomy.
  • The cosmic dawn: Extended deep field observations to probe galaxies at z > 15–20, pushing toward the theoretical epoch of the very first stars (Population III stars, whose light has not yet been directly detected).
  • Solar System targets: High-resolution infrared spectroscopy of Saturn's moon Enceladus (which has active water plumes), Jupiter's moon Europa, and Mars — all candidates for habitability within our own solar system.
  • Stellar evolution: Detailed observations of stellar nurseries, dying stars, and white dwarf systems to test models of how stars form, live, and end.

"Webb is not just a better camera. It is a time machine. Every image is a photograph of the universe as it was billions of years before the Earth existed. And what it is showing us is that our models of how the universe works need revision."

— Mike Ressler, Webb Project Scientist, NASA Jet Propulsion Laboratory, quoted in Nature (2023)12

Frequently Asked Questions

Has Webb actually disproved the Big Bang theory?
No — and this claim, which circulated widely on social media following Webb's early galaxy discoveries, is a significant misrepresentation of the science. The Big Bang model — the universe began in an extremely hot, dense state approximately 13.8 billion years ago and has been expanding and cooling ever since — remains firmly supported by multiple independent lines of evidence including the Cosmic Microwave Background, the abundance of light elements, and the large-scale structure of the universe. What Webb has found is that specific models of how galaxies form within that framework — particularly the Lambda-CDM model's predictions for the earliest galaxies — may need revision. Finding that the universe is more structured in its first few hundred million years than models predicted is a problem for galaxy formation models, not for Big Bang cosmology itself. Science advances by finding where models fail and refining them — this is exactly what Webb is enabling.
How does Webb take colour images if it sees in infrared?
Webb's instruments detect different bands of infrared light, each assigned a different colour for visual representation. The false-colour images released by NASA are scientific data visualisations: each wavelength band is assigned a colour (typically blue, green, and red) and composited. The colours in a Webb image do not correspond to what the objects would look like to human eyes — they represent specific infrared wavelength bands that encode information about temperature, chemical composition, and distance. Different colours can represent different elements (ionised hydrogen glows at specific wavelengths, oxygen at others) or different temperature regions. The image processing team at STScI (Space Telescope Science Institute) makes aesthetic and scientific choices about which wavelength bands to assign to which colours, guided by both scientific utility and visual interpretability.
Can Webb actually detect signs of life on another planet?
Webb has the capability to search for specific atmospheric molecules that could be associated with biological processes — a field called astrobiology. The most discussed biosignatures include oxygen (O₂), ozone (O₃), methane (CH₄), and nitrous oxide (N₂O) in combinations that would be difficult to explain by purely geological or chemical processes. Webb's ability to perform transmission spectroscopy on rocky exoplanet atmospheres is real — it has already characterised WASP-39b and begun studying the TRAPPIST system. However, several caveats apply: Webb can only study the nearest habitable-zone rocky planets (the TRAPPIST system is the prime target); detecting a full biosignature combination rather than a single molecule requires extensive observation time; and the absence of a clear abiotic explanation is not equivalent to confirmation of life. Any detection of a potential biosignature would require independent confirmation by future telescopes. The more accurate framing: Webb could find evidence that warrants much closer investigation, not that it is likely to definitively confirm extraterrestrial life.
Why did the James Webb Space Telescope take so long and cost so much?
Webb's development began in the late 1990s and was originally expected to cost around $1 billion and launch around 2007. The final cost was approximately $10 billion and it launched in December 2021. The delays and cost overruns stemmed from several compounding factors: the unprecedented technical complexity of the 18-segment gold-plated beryllium mirror (which had to unfold in space with submillimetre precision), the development of entirely new cryogenic detector technology for the MIRI instrument (which had to be cooled to 7 Kelvin), the five-layer sunshield (which had to unfold in a specific sequence of 344 steps), and persistent management challenges in the early phases of the programme. A 2018 independent review found that contractor mismanagement and insufficient schedule margin had contributed significantly to overruns. The telescope has nonetheless delivered on its scientific promises — and the consensus among astronomers is that, whatever its cost, it is transforming our understanding of the universe.
