Nuclear Energy Explained — The Complete Science

How nuclear fission actually works, why Chernobyl and Fukushima happened, what makes modern reactor designs fundamentally safer, and what the evidence says about nuclear power's role in addressing climate change.

Nuclear power generation — Nuclear energy currently supplies approximately 9% of the world's electricity from around 440 operating reactors across 32 countries, generating power with one of the lowest carbon footprints and highest energy densities of any electricity source.

Share

On 2 December 1942, a team led by physicist Enrico Fermi achieved the world's first controlled, self-sustaining nuclear chain reaction beneath a squash court at the University of Chicago. The reactor, called Chicago Pile-1, was built from graphite blocks and uranium and produced approximately half a watt of power — barely enough to light a small bulb. It was, nonetheless, the moment humanity first demonstrated that the energy locked inside the atomic nucleus could be released and controlled rather than simply unleashed in an uncontrolled explosion.1

Eighty-three years later, nuclear power generates roughly 9% of the world's electricity and stands at the centre of one of the most polarised debates in energy policy. It is simultaneously the lowest-carbon, most energy-dense source of reliable electricity available at scale, and an industry permanently shadowed by the memory of Chernobyl and Fukushima. This article explains the physics of how nuclear power actually works, why those two accidents happened, what has changed in reactor design since, and what the evidence says about nuclear energy's place in the fight against climate change.

What this article covers: The physics of nuclear fission, how reactors convert that physics into electricity, the causes of the major historical accidents, what modern reactor designs do differently, and a comparison of nuclear's safety and emissions record against other major energy sources.

The Physics — What Nuclear Fission Actually Is

Nuclear fission is the splitting of a heavy atomic nucleus into two lighter nuclei, releasing a significant amount of energy in the process. The fuel used in most commercial reactors is uranium-235, an isotope of uranium whose nucleus is unstable enough to split when struck by a neutron. When a U-235 nucleus absorbs a neutron, it becomes briefly more unstable, then splits into two smaller nuclei, releasing additional neutrons and a substantial amount of energy — primarily as kinetic energy of the fission fragments, which manifests as heat.2

The energy released comes from Einstein's famous relationship E=mc²: the combined mass of the fission products is very slightly less than the mass of the original uranium nucleus and neutron, and that missing mass is converted directly into energy. The amount is staggering by chemical standards — the complete fission of one kilogram of uranium-235 releases approximately as much energy as burning 1.5 million kilograms of coal.3

The neutrons released by each fission event can go on to strike other U-235 nuclei, triggering further fissions in a chain reaction. Whether this chain reaction grows, shrinks, or remains stable depends on how many of the released neutrons go on to cause further fissions — a parameter physicists call criticality. A nuclear reactor is, in essence, a carefully engineered system for sustaining a controlled, stable chain reaction and capturing the resulting heat.

How a Reactor Turns Fission Into Electricity

1
The Core and Control Rods

The reactor core contains nuclear fuel — typically uranium dioxide pellets enriched to 3–5% U-235, arranged in long fuel rods bundled into assemblies. Interspersed among the fuel are control rods made of neutron-absorbing materials such as boron or cadmium. Inserting the control rods further into the core absorbs more neutrons, slowing the chain reaction; withdrawing them allows more neutrons to cause fissions, speeding it up.4

A reactor operating at a stable power level is precisely "critical" — exactly one neutron from each fission goes on to cause another fission, on average. Control rods allow operators to fine-tune this balance continuously.
2
The Coolant Loop

Heat from the fission reaction is carried away by a coolant — in most commercial reactors, ordinary water under high pressure. In a Pressurised Water Reactor (PWR), the most common design worldwide, this primary coolant loop is sealed and never directly contacts the turbine; instead, it heats a secondary loop of water into steam through a heat exchanger.

The steam from the secondary loop drives a conventional steam turbine connected to a generator — mechanically, this final stage is identical to a coal or gas power plant. The fundamental difference between nuclear and fossil fuel power generation is entirely in how the heat is produced, not in how that heat is converted to electricity.5

3
Multiple Independent Safety Barriers

Modern reactor design relies on the principle of "defence in depth" — multiple independent physical barriers between the radioactive fuel and the environment. The fuel itself is encased in a ceramic pellet that retains most fission products. The pellets sit inside a sealed metal fuel rod. The fuel rods sit inside the reactor pressure vessel, a steel structure typically 15–30cm thick. The pressure vessel sits inside a containment building, usually reinforced concrete designed to withstand significant internal pressure and external impact.6

Two Case Studies: What Actually Went Wrong

Case Study 01 — Chernobyl, 26 April 1986

A Design Flaw Combined With a Catastrophic Safety Test

The Chernobyl disaster occurred during a late-night safety test intended to verify whether the reactor's turbines could continue supplying emergency power for long enough to allow backup generators to start during a power loss. The test required operators to deliberately reduce reactor power to a low level, but the RBMK-1000 reactor design used at Chernobyl had a dangerous characteristic at low power: a positive void coefficient, meaning that as cooling water turned to steam, reactivity increased rather than decreased — the opposite of the inherently self-stabilising behaviour found in Western reactor designs.7

Compounding this design flaw, operators had withdrawn far more control rods than safety procedures allowed in order to keep the reactor running during the test, leaving inadequate margin to shut the reaction down quickly if needed. When a power surge began, the emergency shutdown procedure itself made the situation catastrophically worse: the control rods' graphite tips, due to a separate design flaw, briefly increased reactivity as they were inserted before beginning to suppress it. The resulting power spike, estimated at up to 100 times normal output within seconds, ruptured fuel channels and triggered a steam explosion that destroyed the reactor core and blew the roof off the reactor building, releasing massive quantities of radioactive material directly into the atmosphere.

The United Nations Scientific Committee on the Effects of Atomic Radiation has attributed approximately 50 confirmed deaths directly to the accident's acute radiation effects, primarily among emergency responders, with estimates of eventual excess cancer deaths from the radioactive release ranging from several hundred to several thousand depending on methodology — a figure that remains scientifically contested. The accident led to the permanent evacuation of an exclusion zone that remains largely uninhabited today.8

Key lesson: Chernobyl resulted from a reactor design with an inherent instability at low power, combined with operators violating safety procedures during an unauthorised test, combined with a flawed emergency shutdown mechanism. No Western commercial reactor design has shared the RBMK's positive void coefficient.
Case Study 02 — Fukushima Daiichi, 11 March 2011

A Natural Disaster That Exposed an Inadequate Safety Margin

The Fukushima Daiichi accident began when the Tohoku earthquake, the most powerful ever recorded in Japan, triggered an automatic shutdown of the plant's reactors — the safety systems worked exactly as designed. The catastrophic failure came roughly 50 minutes later, when a tsunami with waves reaching approximately 14 metres overwhelmed the plant's 5.7-metre seawall, flooding the emergency diesel generators that were needed to power the cooling systems for the now-shutdown but still extremely hot reactor cores.9

Without power for cooling, the reactor cores in three of the plant's units overheated, and the fuel began to melt. The melting fuel reacted with the remaining cooling water to produce hydrogen gas, which subsequently exploded in three reactor buildings, damaging their outer structures, though the primary containment vessels largely held. Radioactive material was released, primarily into the surrounding ocean and atmosphere, leading to the evacuation of approximately 150,000 people from the surrounding area.

Unlike Chernobyl, there were no direct deaths from radiation exposure at Fukushima. The World Health Organization's detailed assessment found no discernible increase in cancer rates attributable to radiation exposure for the general population, though it identified a small increased risk for some emergency workers with the highest exposures. The Japanese government's own investigation concluded the disaster was fundamentally a failure of risk assessment and regulatory oversight — the tsunami risk had been understated despite available geological evidence of historical tsunamis of comparable or greater height in the region.10

Key lesson: Fukushima was caused by an inadequately sized seawall and a single point of failure — loss of all power sources for cooling — rather than any flaw in the fission process itself. Post-Fukushima reactor designs now mandate multiple, physically separated, redundant backup power and cooling systems specifically to prevent this failure mode.

Modern Reactor Designs — What Has Changed

Generation III+ reactors, now being built worldwide, incorporate passive safety systems that do not depend on active power or human intervention to prevent meltdown. The AP1000 design, for example, uses gravity-fed water tanks and natural convection cooling that activate automatically and can keep the reactor safe for 72 hours without any external power or operator action — directly addressing the failure mode that caused Fukushima.11

Small Modular Reactors (SMRs), an emerging category, are designed to be factory-built, transported to site, and assembled rather than constructed entirely on location — potentially reducing both cost overruns and construction time, which have historically plagued large nuclear projects. Several SMR designs incorporate inherent safety features, such as fuel and coolant configurations that are physically incapable of sustaining a runaway chain reaction even with all active safety systems disabled.

This data, compiled from Our World in Data using sources including the World Health Organization and peer-reviewed life-cycle assessments, consistently shows nuclear power among the safest and lowest-carbon major energy sources when measured per unit of electricity actually delivered — a finding that surprises most people given nuclear's prominence in public anxiety about energy safety. Public perception of nuclear risk is shaped disproportionately by two highly visible accidents over six decades of operation, rather than by the comparative statistical safety record across hundreds of reactor-years of operation.12

"Nuclear power's worst-case scenarios are dramatic and visible. Fossil fuel's worst-case scenario is invisible, distributed, and ongoing — it is simply called air pollution, and it kills millions of people every year without ever making international headlines."

— Adapted from public health and energy systems literature comparing visible versus diffuse energy harms13

The honest synthesis: nuclear power carries genuine risks that require rigorous, continuously improving safety regulation, and the consequences of failure, while statistically rare, can be severe and long-lasting in specific locations. At the same time, the evidence on aggregate harm per unit of energy delivered consistently places nuclear among the safest major energy sources, and its near-zero operational carbon emissions make it relevant to discussions of how to meet growing global electricity demand while reducing greenhouse gas emissions.

Frequently Asked Questions

Could a nuclear reactor explode like a nuclear bomb?
No. Nuclear weapons require uranium enriched to approximately 90% U-235, or plutonium of similarly high purity, assembled in a precise geometric configuration to achieve a supercritical mass within microseconds. Commercial reactor fuel is enriched to only 3–5% U-235 — far too dilute to sustain the kind of explosive, near-instantaneous chain reaction a weapon requires. The "explosions" that occurred at Chernobyl and Fukushima were steam and hydrogen explosions caused by extreme heat reacting with water or zirconium fuel cladding, not nuclear detonations. This is a fundamentally different physical process from a nuclear weapon, governed by chemistry and thermodynamics rather than the physics of a supercritical chain reaction.
What happens to nuclear waste?
Used nuclear fuel is highly radioactive and requires careful long-term management. It is initially stored in cooling pools at the reactor site for several years to allow short-lived radioactive isotopes to decay and the heat output to decrease. After this, it is typically transferred to dry cask storage — sealed steel and concrete containers designed to safely contain the material for decades. The long-term solution favoured by most nuclear engineers and several countries, including Finland and Sweden, is deep geological repositories — stable rock formations several hundred metres underground designed to isolate the waste for the tens of thousands of years required for radioactivity to decay to safe levels. Finland's Onkalo facility became the world's first operational deep geological repository for spent nuclear fuel. A frequently overlooked point is that nuclear waste volume is extremely small relative to energy produced — all the high-level waste produced by the US nuclear industry since the 1950s would fit on a single football field stacked less than ten metres high.
Why does building new nuclear power take so long and cost so much?
Recent nuclear construction projects in Western countries, including Vogtle in the United States and Hinkley Point C in the United Kingdom, have experienced significant cost overruns and delays of several years compared to initial projections. The reasons are debated among energy economists but generally include the loss of construction expertise and supply chains after decades of reduced nuclear building, the complexity of first-of-a-kind designs for new reactor types, evolving regulatory requirements during multi-year construction periods, and the sheer scale and precision required for nuclear-grade construction. By contrast, countries that have maintained continuous nuclear construction programmes, including South Korea and historically France, have generally achieved faster build times and lower costs by maintaining standardised designs and experienced workforces across successive projects, suggesting the cost and schedule problems are substantially attributable to inconsistent construction pipelines rather than to anything inherent in nuclear technology itself.
Is nuclear energy considered renewable or sustainable?
Nuclear energy is not classified as renewable in the technical sense, since it relies on a finite resource — mined uranium — rather than naturally replenishing sources like sunlight or wind. However, uranium reserves are extensive relative to current usage, and breeder reactor technology, which converts non-fissile uranium-238 into fissile plutonium, could theoretically extend usable nuclear fuel supplies for many centuries at current consumption rates, though breeder reactors remain a relatively small part of current global capacity. Whether nuclear should be considered "sustainable" in the broader sense of supporting long-term human and environmental wellbeing is a separate question from the renewable classification, and many climate scientists and energy policy researchers argue nuclear's near-zero operational carbon emissions and high energy density make it an important sustainability tool regardless of the renewable/non-renewable technical distinction.
