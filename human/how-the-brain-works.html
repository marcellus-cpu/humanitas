How the Human Brain Actually Works — A Complete Scientific Explanation

The human brain contains 86 billion neurons, each connected to thousands of others, forming a network more complex than any structure in the known universe. It generates every thought you have ever had, every emotion you have ever felt, and every memory you will ever form. This is an accurate, complete account of how it actually does that.

Right now, as you read this sentence, approximately 86 billion neurons inside your skull are firing electrical signals at each other across roughly 100 trillion synaptic connections. The pattern of those signals — their timing, their strength, their routing through specific brain regions — is what reading is. It is also what understanding is, what curiosity feels like, what recognition of a familiar word means. Every single thing you will do today, from the mundane to the profound, is the product of this three-pound organ generating electrical and chemical activity so complex that no computer on earth comes close to replicating it.1

Most popular explanations of the brain are either dangerously oversimplified — the left brain/right brain myth, the idea that we use only 10% of our brains, the notion that the brain is "just like a computer" — or so technically dense as to be inaccessible. This article attempts something more difficult: an accurate account of how the brain actually works, built on peer-reviewed neuroscience, stripped of the myths, and written for anyone willing to think carefully about the organ doing their thinking.

Three things to know before we start: First, almost everything you think you know about the brain from popular science is wrong or oversimplified. Second, neuroscience is one of the fastest-moving fields in science — what we knew in 2000 has been substantially revised. Third, the brain is not a single organ doing one thing. It is a collection of highly specialised regions doing different things simultaneously and in parallel, coordinated by mechanisms we are still working to understand.

The Basic Unit — What a Neuron Is and What It Does

A neuron is a specialised cell designed to receive, process, and transmit information through electrical and chemical signals. It has three main parts: the dendrites — branching extensions that receive incoming signals from other neurons; the cell body (soma) — which integrates those signals and performs the cell's basic maintenance functions; and the axon — a long fibre that transmits the neuron's output signal to other neurons, sometimes over considerable distances.

The signal itself is an action potential — a rapid, temporary reversal of the electrical charge across the neuron's membrane. At rest, the inside of the neuron is negatively charged relative to the outside. When incoming signals push the voltage above a threshold, sodium ions rush into the cell, generating a rapid positive spike in voltage that travels down the axon like a wave. This all happens in about one millisecond.2

When the action potential reaches the end of the axon, it triggers the release of chemical messengers called neurotransmitters into the synapse — the tiny gap between the axon terminal of one neuron and the dendrite of the next. The neurotransmitters bind to receptors on the receiving neuron, either exciting it (making it more likely to fire) or inhibiting it (making it less likely to fire). The balance of excitation and inhibition across millions of these connections at any moment is what determines what your brain does next.

The key neurotransmitters and their roles: glutamate (the brain's primary excitatory neurotransmitter — involved in learning and memory); GABA (the primary inhibitory neurotransmitter — reduces neuronal excitability throughout the brain); dopamine (reward, motivation, motor control — implicated in addiction, Parkinson's disease, and schizophrenia); serotonin (mood, sleep, appetite — the primary target of antidepressant medications); norepinephrine (alertness, attention, the fight-or-flight response); acetylcholine (muscle activation, memory, attention).3

The Architecture — Six Key Brain Regions and What Each Does

The Cerebral Cortex
Highest cognitive functions

The cerebral cortex is the deeply folded outer layer of the brain — the grey, wrinkled surface visible in any photograph of a brain. It is approximately 2–4 millimetres thick but, if unfolded, would cover an area of about 2,500 square centimetres. The folding (convolutions) dramatically increases surface area within the confined space of the skull.4

The cortex is divided into four lobes. The frontal lobe — the largest — handles decision-making, planning, impulse control, working memory, and the inhibition of inappropriate behaviours. It is the most recently evolved part of the human brain and the most distinctively human. The parietal lobe integrates sensory information and handles spatial awareness. The temporal lobe processes auditory information, language comprehension, and is critical for long-term memory formation. The occipital lobe is dedicated almost entirely to visual processing.

What makes humans distinctive: The prefrontal cortex — the front section of the frontal lobe — is proportionally much larger in humans than in any other primate. It is the seat of abstract thinking, moral reasoning, long-term planning, and what we call personality. It is also the last brain region to fully mature — not until approximately age 25.
The Hippocampus
Memory formation

The hippocampus — named for its resemblance to a seahorse — is a curved structure buried deep in the temporal lobe. It is essential for converting short-term memories into long-term ones, a process called memory consolidation. Without a functioning hippocampus, new experiences cannot be stored as lasting memories, though memories formed before hippocampal damage remain intact.

The most dramatic demonstration of hippocampal function comes from the patient known as H.M. — Henry Molaison — who had both hippocampi surgically removed in 1953 to treat severe epilepsy. He was left completely unable to form new long-term memories for the remaining 55 years of his life. He could hold a conversation but would not remember having met you minutes later. Every day was, for him, an experience without context — he genuinely could not remember what he had done that morning. His case defined hippocampal function for a generation of neuroscientists.5

The hippocampus also contains the brain's "GPS system" — place cells and grid cells that track your location in space. John O'Keefe and the Mosers won the 2014 Nobel Prize in Physiology for this discovery.
The Amygdala
Emotion and threat detection

The amygdala — two almond-shaped structures, one in each hemisphere — is the brain's primary threat-detection and emotional processing centre. It receives sensory information faster than the cortex does, enabling rapid fear responses before conscious awareness catches up. When you flinch at a sudden loud noise before you have consciously identified it, that is your amygdala acting ahead of your cortex.6

The amygdala is central to the formation of emotionally charged memories. This is why you remember exactly where you were during a frightening or emotionally intense event — the amygdala signals the hippocampus to encode that memory with particular strength. It is also why people with amygdala damage fail to show normal fear responses and cannot recognise fearful expressions in others' faces.

Post-traumatic stress disorder involves dysregulation of the amygdala — it continues generating fear responses to stimuli associated with past trauma, even when those stimuli are no longer dangerous, because the normal extinction of fear memories is impaired.
The Cerebellum
Movement and coordination

The cerebellum — literally "little brain" — sits at the back of the brain below the cerebral cortex. It contains approximately 50% of all the neurons in the brain despite accounting for only about 10% of its volume. Its primary function is coordinating movement, balance, and fine motor control — it receives information about intended movements from the motor cortex and sensory feedback from the body, then fine-tunes the execution to make movement smooth and accurate.7

Damage to the cerebellum produces ataxia — uncoordinated, lurching movement that resembles intoxication. Alcohol suppresses cerebellar function, which is why drunk people stagger and fail sobriety tests. The cerebellum is also increasingly understood to play roles in cognitive and emotional processing beyond movement — its connectivity with the prefrontal cortex suggests functions in working memory and attention that are still being mapped.

The Prefrontal Cortex
Decision-making and impulse control

The prefrontal cortex (PFC) deserves special attention because it is the region most responsible for what makes us distinctively human. It handles executive functions: planning, decision-making, weighing consequences, inhibiting impulses, regulating emotions, and maintaining goals in working memory. It is also the region that allows us to imagine the future, consider hypothetical scenarios, and reflect on our own mental states — a capacity called metacognition.8

The PFC is the last region of the brain to complete development, reaching full maturity around age 25. This explains why adolescents and young adults show characteristically higher risk-taking, impulsivity, and sensitivity to peer influence — their prefrontal cortex is literally not yet fully functional. It also has significant implications for criminal justice: the underdeveloped PFC of adolescent offenders is now a legally relevant factor in sentencing in several jurisdictions.

Phineas Gage, a railway worker whose prefrontal cortex was destroyed by an iron rod in an 1848 accident, survived but underwent a complete personality change — from reliable and responsible to impulsive, profane, and unable to follow plans. His case was the first evidence that the PFC controls personality and social behaviour.

How Memory Actually Works — The Four Types

Memory is not a single system. The brain uses at least four distinct memory systems, each involving different brain structures and mechanisms.

Working memory is the brain's mental scratchpad — the temporary storage of information currently being used or manipulated. It allows you to hold a phone number in mind long enough to dial it, or to keep the beginning of a sentence in mind while processing the end. Working memory is tightly linked to the prefrontal cortex and has a very limited capacity — approximately 4–7 items at once. When you lose your train of thought mid-sentence, it is your working memory that has failed.9

Episodic memory is memory for specific personal experiences — events that happened to you at a particular time and place. It is what you access when you remember your last birthday, the smell of a childhood home, or a conversation from last week. The hippocampus is essential for encoding episodic memories, which are then gradually consolidated and distributed across cortical networks during sleep.

Semantic memory is memory for facts and general knowledge — what Paris is the capital of, what a bicycle is, how to conjugate a verb. It is largely independent of the hippocampus once established, which is why patients with severe hippocampal damage (like H.M.) retain their general knowledge while losing the ability to form new episodic memories.

Procedural memory is memory for skills and habits — how to ride a bicycle, type on a keyboard, play a chord on a guitar. It is stored primarily in the cerebellum and basal ganglia rather than the hippocampus, which explains why patients with severe amnesia can still learn new skills even though they have no memory of the learning sessions themselves. H.M. gradually improved at mirror-drawing tasks across sessions despite having no recollection of ever having attempted them.10

Two Case Studies: When the Brain Breaks — and What It Reveals

Case Study 01 — Phineas Gage and the Personality of the Frontal Lobe

The Accident That Revealed Where Personality Lives in the Brain

On 13 September 1848, Phineas Gage was working as a foreman on a Vermont railway construction site when an accidental explosion drove a tamping iron — a metal rod 109 centimetres long and 3 centimetres in diameter — through the left side of his face, behind his left eye, through his skull, and out the top of his head. The rod landed some 25 metres away, covered in brain matter.

Gage survived. He was conscious within minutes. He could speak and walk and remember who he was. His physical recovery was, by the standards of 1848, remarkable. But those who knew him before the accident described a man transformed. The Gage who had been responsible, hard-working, and well-liked became — in the words of his doctor John Harlow — "fitful, irreverent, indulging at times in the grossest profanity, manifesting but little deference for his fellows, impatient of restraint or advice when it conflicts with his desires, at times pertinaciously obstinate, yet capricious and vacillating." His employers, who had considered him the most capable of their foremen before the injury, would not give him his position back. His friends said he was "no longer Gage."11

Modern reconstruction of Gage's injury using his preserved skull and computer modelling has confirmed that the rod destroyed primarily the ventromedial prefrontal cortex — the region now known to be critical for social behaviour, emotional regulation, and decision-making in social contexts. Gage's case was the first clear evidence that personality — the consistent patterns of behaviour and social conduct we consider essential to a person's identity — has a specific neurological substrate that can be damaged.

Key insight: Personality is not an abstract quality. It is a product of specific neural circuits, primarily in the prefrontal cortex. Damage those circuits and the person you were is, in a meaningful sense, gone — replaced by someone who looks the same but behaves differently.
Case Study 02 — Neuroplasticity in London Taxi Drivers

How Learning Physically Changes the Structure of Your Brain

London taxi drivers must pass "The Knowledge" — a notoriously demanding examination requiring candidates to memorise 25,000 streets and 20,000 landmarks within a 10-kilometre radius of Charing Cross. Preparation takes an average of four years of intensive study. In 2000, Eleanor Maguire and colleagues at University College London published a landmark study comparing the brains of licensed London taxi drivers to those of non-taxi drivers using MRI scanning.

The finding was striking: the posterior hippocampus — the region associated with spatial navigation and the formation of cognitive maps — was significantly larger in taxi drivers than in controls, and the increase was correlated with the number of years spent driving. Further analysis showed that the anterior hippocampus was slightly smaller in drivers, suggesting a reorganisation rather than a simple enlargement. Taxi drivers who had retired showed that the posterior hippocampus gradually returned toward normal size after they stopped navigating.12

This study was among the most influential demonstrations of adult neuroplasticity — the brain's capacity to physically change its structure in response to learning and experience. The adult brain, once thought to be essentially fixed after early development, is now known to continuously modify its connections, grow new synapses, and in some regions even generate new neurons in response to experience. This has profound implications for education, rehabilitation after brain injury, and the treatment of mental illness.

Key insight: The brain is not fixed. Every significant learning experience physically changes it. This means that education, practice, and deliberate effort are not just behaviourally effective — they are biologically transformative.

Consciousness — The Hardest Problem

Everything described so far — neurons, regions, memory systems, action potentials — is the "easy" part of neuroscience, in the sense that we can observe and measure it. The genuinely hard problem is consciousness: why any of this physical activity feels like something from the inside. Why does the firing of neurons in your visual cortex not just process light wavelengths but produce the experience of redness? Why does activity in your limbic system not just modulate behaviour but feel like sadness?

Philosopher David Chalmers, who coined the term "the hard problem of consciousness," argued that neuroscience can explain how the brain processes information but cannot, even in principle, explain why that processing is accompanied by subjective experience. We currently have no scientific theory that successfully explains how physical brain processes give rise to conscious experience.13

The leading scientific frameworks include Integrated Information Theory (IIT), proposed by Giulio Tononi, which holds that consciousness is identical to a specific kind of information processing; Global Workspace Theory, proposed by Bernard Baars, which holds that consciousness arises when information is broadcast widely across brain regions from a "global workspace"; and predictive processing frameworks that understand consciousness as the brain's model of itself. None has achieved consensus. The relationship between the brain and the mind remains the deepest unsolved problem in science.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do we really only use 10% of our brains?
No — this is one of the most persistent myths in popular neuroscience and it is completely false. Brain imaging studies consistently show that virtually all brain regions are active in the course of a normal day, and that even during sleep most of the brain remains active. The myth likely originated from a misquotation or misinterpretation of early neuroscience research, possibly related to the finding that only about 10% of brain cells are neurons (the rest are glial cells, which perform essential supporting functions). The evolutionary cost of maintaining a brain — it consumes 20% of the body's energy despite being 2% of its weight — makes it essentially impossible that 90% of it would be kept running uselessly. Every region of the brain serves a function, and damage to virtually any region produces measurable deficits.
Is it true that the left brain is logical and the right brain is creative?
This is largely a myth — a severe oversimplification of real neuroscience. The left and right hemispheres do have some specialisations: language production is predominantly left-lateralised in most right-handed people, and certain spatial tasks tend to be more right-lateralised. But the idea that people are either "left-brained" (analytical) or "right-brained" (creative) has no basis in neuroscience. Brain imaging studies consistently show that both hemispheres are involved in almost all cognitive tasks. Creative thinking, for example, activates networks spanning both hemispheres. The two hemispheres communicate continuously through a thick band of fibres called the corpus callosum, and in normal brains they function as a highly integrated unit, not as two separate processors.
Can the adult brain grow new neurons?
This is genuinely contested in current neuroscience. The traditional view held that neurogenesis — the growth of new neurons — essentially stopped after early childhood in humans. Research in the 1990s and 2000s suggested that the hippocampus was an exception, showing ongoing neurogenesis in adults. However, more recent studies using improved methods have reached contradictory conclusions — some finding robust adult hippocampal neurogenesis, others finding little to none in human tissue. The current consensus is uncertain: adult neurogenesis almost certainly occurs in some brain regions in some mammals, but whether it occurs at meaningful rates in the adult human brain remains actively debated. What is not debated is adult neuroplasticity — the modification of existing synaptic connections — which clearly occurs throughout life.
How does sleep affect the brain?
Sleep is not a passive resting state — it is a period of intense and essential brain activity. During sleep, the brain consolidates memories formed during waking hours, transferring information from the hippocampus to long-term cortical storage. It also clears metabolic waste products — including amyloid beta, the protein that accumulates in Alzheimer's disease — through the glymphatic system, a waste-clearance mechanism that operates primarily during sleep. Different sleep stages serve different functions: slow-wave (deep) sleep is associated with declarative memory consolidation; REM sleep with emotional memory processing and procedural learning. Chronic sleep deprivation is associated with impaired memory, reduced executive function, increased emotional reactivity, and a higher long-term risk of neurodegenerative disease.
