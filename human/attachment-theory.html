What Attachment Theory Reveals About Every Relationship You Have Ever Had

The way you learned to relate to your first caregivers created a template your nervous system still uses in every close relationship you have as an adult. Attachment theory — now one of the most empirically robust frameworks in developmental psychology — explains the patterns. Here is the full science.

In 1958, a psychologist named Harry Harlow published the results of a series of experiments with infant rhesus monkeys that would change the course of developmental psychology. Harlow separated infant monkeys from their mothers and gave them two surrogate "mothers": one made of wire that dispensed milk, and one made of soft terrycloth that provided no food whatsoever. According to the prevailing behavioural theory of the time — which held that infants attached to caregivers because caregivers provided food — the infants should have bonded with the wire mother. They did not. They clung to the cloth mother for comfort, visiting the wire mother only briefly to feed, and ran to the cloth mother when frightened.1

Harlow's experiment demolished the "cupboard love" theory of attachment and opened the way for the theoretical framework that John Bowlby was already developing in parallel — a framework that has since become one of the most empirically studied and practically consequential theories in all of psychology. Attachment theory holds that human beings are biologically primed to form strong emotional bonds with specific caregivers, that the quality of those early bonds creates internal working models of relationships that persist into adulthood, and that these models shape every close relationship we subsequently form.

What has made attachment theory endure — and what distinguishes it from the many psychological frameworks that have come and gone — is the sheer volume of empirical research it has generated and survived. Over 10,000 studies have been published on attachment since Bowlby's first major work in 1969. The core findings have been replicated across cultures, developmental stages, and relationship types. The theory is not without its critics and revisions, but it is among the most robust frameworks available for understanding why people relate to each other the way they do.

Bowlby and the Origins: What Attachment Theory Actually Claims

John Bowlby, a British psychiatrist and psychoanalyst, developed attachment theory across three major volumes published between 1969 and 1980: Attachment, Separation, and Loss. His central argument was evolutionary: human infants are born entirely helpless and require sustained parental care to survive. Natural selection would therefore strongly favour any mechanism that kept infants close to protective caregivers and motivated caregivers to provide protection. The attachment system is that mechanism.2

The attachment system works through a simple behavioural logic. When an infant feels threatened — by separation, fear, hunger, or pain — the system activates and drives proximity-seeking behaviour: crying, clinging, reaching. When proximity to the caregiver is achieved and the caregiver responds sensitively, the system is deactivated and the infant can return to exploring its environment. The caregiver functions as a "secure base" from which exploration can safely proceed and a "safe haven" to which the child can return when threatened.

What determines attachment security, in Bowlby's model, is not the amount of care but its consistency and sensitivity. A caregiver who responds reliably, promptly, and appropriately to an infant's signals creates the conditions for secure attachment. A caregiver who responds inconsistently, dismissively, or intrusively creates the conditions for insecure attachment in its various forms.3

Mary Ainsworth and the Strange Situation

The theoretical edifice Bowlby constructed needed an empirical foundation, and that foundation was provided by Mary Ainsworth's Strange Situation procedure — one of the most replicated experimental paradigms in developmental psychology. Ainsworth developed the procedure in the 1960s and 1970s to directly observe the quality of infant-caregiver attachment by putting infants through a series of brief, mildly stressful separations and reunions.4

In the Strange Situation, a mother and infant (typically 12–18 months) are placed in a room with toys. A stranger enters. The mother leaves briefly, then returns. The mother leaves again, this time leaving the infant alone. The stranger returns. The mother returns. The procedure is designed to activate the attachment system progressively and observe how the infant uses the caregiver upon reunion.

From the Strange Situation, Ainsworth identified three primary infant attachment patterns, later expanded to four by Main and Solomon:

Style 01 · ~55–65% of population
Secure Attachment
Caregiver was: Consistent, sensitive, responsive

In infancy: distressed at separation, visibly relieved and easily soothed at reunion. Uses caregiver as secure base for exploration.

In adult relationships: Comfortable with intimacy and interdependence. Able to depend on others without anxiety. Able to be depended on. Communicates needs directly. Manages conflict constructively. Regulates emotions effectively.

The internal working model: "I am worthy of love. Others are reliable. Close relationships are safe."

Style 02 · ~15–20% of population
Anxious / Preoccupied
Caregiver was: Inconsistent — sometimes responsive, sometimes not

In infancy: extremely distressed at separation, not easily soothed at reunion — continuing to cry and cling even after the caregiver returns. Difficulty returning to exploration.

In adult relationships: Preoccupied with relationship security. Fear of abandonment. Difficulty feeling satisfied with the level of closeness. Heightened sensitivity to partner's emotional signals. Tendency to pursue when partner withdraws. Emotional volatility during conflict.

The internal working model: "I may not be worthy of consistent love. I must monitor and maintain connection vigilantly."

Style 03 · ~20–25% of population
Avoidant / Dismissing
Caregiver was: Consistently emotionally unavailable or rejecting

In infancy: appears not distressed at separation. Ignores or avoids caregiver at reunion. Elevated cortisol levels revealed internal stress despite behavioural calm.

In adult relationships: Strong emphasis on self-reliance and independence. Uncomfortable with emotional intimacy. Tendency to suppress emotional needs and withdraw when relationship demands increase. Positive view of self, sceptical view of others' reliability.

The internal working model: "I am self-sufficient. Depending on others is unsafe. Closeness leads to disappointment."

Style 04 · ~15–20% of population
Disorganised / Fearful
Caregiver was: Frightening, frightened, or abusive

In infancy: disorganised, contradictory behaviour at reunion — approaching and then freezing, or displaying odd repetitive movements. The caregiver is simultaneously the source of fear and the solution to it.

In adult relationships: Simultaneous desire for and fear of intimacy. Difficulty regulating emotions in relationship contexts. Higher rates of trauma histories. Relationship patterns that oscillate between idealisation and devaluation.

The internal working model: "I want closeness and I am terrified of it. Love and danger are connected."

The Transmission to Adult Relationships

The claim that infant attachment patterns predict adult relationship behaviour is the most contested and most consequential aspect of attachment theory. The empirical answer is: yes, moderately and probabilistically — not deterministically.

Mary Main, who extended Ainsworth's work into adult attachment research, developed the Adult Attachment Interview (AAI) — a semi-structured interview examining how adults narrate and make sense of their childhood attachment experiences. Main found that the coherence of an adult's narrative about their childhood — not the content of the experiences themselves — predicted the attachment security of their own children with striking consistency. Parents who could discuss difficult childhood experiences with coherence, compassion, and integration tended to have securely attached children. Parents whose narratives were dismissive, incoherent, or unresolved tended to have insecurely attached children.5

This finding has two important implications. First, it is not the experiences themselves that transmit attachment patterns across generations — it is how those experiences are processed and integrated. A person who had a difficult childhood but has made meaning of it coherently can parent securely. Second, and consequently, attachment patterns can change.

The earned security finding: Longitudinal research has identified a group of adults who were classified as insecurely attached in infancy but securely attached as adults — people who have developed what researchers call "earned security" through corrective relationship experiences, therapy, or life events that provided new information about the reliability of close others. This group is empirically distinguishable from those with "continuous security" (secure across development) but shows similar adult relationship outcomes — demonstrating that early attachment is influential but not destiny.6

The Neuroscience of Attachment: What Brain Imaging Shows

The arrival of neuroimaging technology has allowed researchers to examine the neural basis of attachment security — moving the theory from behavioural observation to biological mechanism. The findings are consistent with the theory and reveal the depth at which attachment patterns are encoded.

Securely attached adults, when viewing distressing images, show different patterns of amygdala activation than insecurely attached adults. Their emotional regulation circuits engage more efficiently — the prefrontal cortex modulates the amygdala's threat response more effectively, allowing them to experience distress without being overwhelmed by it.7 This neurological difference is the biological substrate of what attachment researchers call "affect regulation" — the capacity to experience and manage emotions without either suppressing them (avoidant) or being dysregulated by them (anxious).

The oxytocin system — discussed in the article on the neuroscience of romantic love — is centrally implicated in attachment. Oxytocin receptor density in the nucleus accumbens is associated with proximity-seeking and pair bonding behaviour in animal studies; in humans, intranasal oxytocin administration increases trust and approach behaviour in social contexts.8 Research by Riem and colleagues showed that insecure attachment is associated with altered oxytocin responses to infant cries — a finding that partly explains the intergenerational transmission of attachment patterns through caregiving behaviour.9

Two Case Studies: Attachment Patterns in Practice

Case Study 01 — The Anxious-Avoidant Trap

Why Anxious and Avoidant Partners Find Each Other — and Struggle

The most common and most distressing relationship dynamic identified in attachment research is the pairing of an anxiously attached person with an avoidantly attached person. Research consistently finds this pairing is more common than chance would predict — suggesting that the two styles are drawn to each other.10

The dynamic makes sense when understood through the lens of internal working models. The anxiously attached person, whose model says "I must pursue connection vigilantly," finds the avoidant partner initially attractive — they are often confident, self-contained, and emotionally composed in ways that feel reassuringly stable. The avoidant person, whose model says "closeness is dangerous but I want it," finds the anxious partner's persistence initially flattering and their evident emotional investment reassuring.

The conflict emerges in the attachment system's activation. When the anxious partner feels insecure — which happens readily, given their model — they pursue: more contact, more reassurance, more emotional expression. This activates the avoidant partner's deactivating strategy — they withdraw to manage the intimacy demand. The withdrawal confirms the anxious partner's fear of abandonment and intensifies the pursuit. The pursuit confirms the avoidant partner's expectation that intimacy leads to overwhelming demands and intensifies the withdrawal.

This is the anxious-avoidant cycle — a self-reinforcing loop that plays out in an infinite variety of specific arguments but always has the same underlying structure. The insight from attachment theory is that neither partner is "wrong" or "broken": each is following a strategy that made sense given their early experiences and that is now causing problems in a different context.

Key finding: The anxious-avoidant pairing is over-represented in distressed relationships. Both partners' behaviour makes sense as attachment strategy. The therapeutic target is not changing who someone is but changing the meaning attributed to the partner's behaviour.
Case Study 02 — The Van IJzendoorn Meta-Analysis

How Reliably Do Infant Attachment Patterns Predict Adult Relationships?

Marinus van IJzendoorn's 1995 meta-analysis, examining 18 studies with a combined sample of over 850 parent-infant dyads, confirmed what had been suggested by smaller studies: parents' attachment representations — as measured by the Adult Attachment Interview — predicted their infants' Strange Situation classifications with remarkable consistency. The correspondence rate was approximately 75%.11

Crucially, van IJzendoorn also identified what he called the "transmission gap" — the observation that even the studies showing a strong correspondence could not fully explain how parental attachment representation affected infant security. It was not simply through behaviour: parents' attachment representations predicted infant security even when parental sensitivity ratings were controlled for. There appeared to be additional transmission mechanisms beyond visible caregiving behaviour — possibly including non-verbal attunement, emotional availability, and subtle physiological synchrony.

This meta-analysis is important because it established the empirical credibility of intergenerational transmission as a real phenomenon rather than clinical anecdote, and because the "transmission gap" it identified has driven two decades of subsequent research into the mechanisms of attachment transmission.

Key finding: Parental attachment security predicts infant attachment security at approximately 75% correspondence — robust but not deterministic. The "transmission gap" suggests mechanisms beyond visible parenting behaviour that are still being investigated.

Can Attachment Style Change? The Evidence

The most practically important question in attachment research is whether adult attachment styles are fixed or changeable — and the evidence is moderately optimistic. Attachment security is best understood not as a fixed trait but as a probabilistic disposition that can be updated by new relational experiences.

The most consistent finding across the literature is that the route to greater attachment security runs through reflective functioning — the capacity to understand that one's own and other people's behaviour is driven by mental states (beliefs, desires, feelings) that are distinct from external reality.16 This is precisely what coherent AAI narratives demonstrate: not the absence of difficult experiences, but the capacity to reflect on them with understanding rather than being overwhelmed or dismissive of them.

"The goal of attachment-informed therapy is not to give clients a different past, but to give them a different relationship to their past — one in which early experiences are understood without being either dismissed or re-enacted."

— Daniel Siegel, Clinical Professor of Psychiatry, UCLA School of Medicine, The Developing Mind (2nd ed., 2012), p. 8917

The honest summary of sixty years of attachment research is this: your early attachment experiences matter — more than most people realise, and more than most schools teach. They created a set of expectations, emotional regulation patterns, and relational strategies that your nervous system still deploys automatically in situations of closeness and vulnerability. These patterns are not destiny. They are, with insight and corrective experience, changeable. But changing them requires more than understanding them intellectually — it requires the lived experience of relating differently, repeatedly, over time.

Frequently Asked Questions

What percentage of people are securely attached?
Meta-analyses of Strange Situation studies consistently find that approximately 55–65% of infants in low-risk Western samples are classified as securely attached, with the remainder split between anxious (approximately 15–20%), avoidant (20–25%), and disorganised (15–20%). These proportions vary across cultural contexts and between high-risk and low-risk samples — higher rates of insecure attachment are found in samples with elevated rates of poverty, abuse, parental mental illness, and other adversities. The proportions also shift somewhat when measured in adult samples, partly because the instruments differ and partly because adult experiences have updated some early patterns. A commonly cited figure for secure attachment in adult populations is approximately 50–60%, though this varies by measurement method.
Is attachment style determined entirely by parenting?
No — and this is one of the important nuances that popular accounts of attachment theory often miss. Attachment security is influenced by parenting quality but also by child temperament, subsequent relationship experiences, socioeconomic stress, and life events. Genetic factors contribute to temperament, which interacts with parenting to influence attachment outcomes. Behavioural genetic studies suggest that heredity accounts for a modest but non-zero proportion of attachment security variance. The influential "differential susceptibility" hypothesis, advanced by Jay Belsky and colleagues, proposes that some children are more reactive to both adverse and positive caregiving environments — the same temperamental qualities that make a child more vulnerable to insensitive parenting also make them more responsive to sensitive parenting.
Can you have different attachment styles in different relationships?
Yes. Attachment theory recognises both a "global" attachment orientation (a general tendency across relationships) and relationship-specific attachment models. You may be generally more anxious or avoidant, but your specific attachment behaviour can differ substantially between relationships — you may be relatively secure with a close friend and more anxious in a romantic partnership. This is because relationship-specific models are updated by specific relational experiences with that particular person. The global orientation provides the default starting point; specific relationships update it in specific directions. This also means that a highly secure relationship experience can shift someone's behaviour in that specific relationship even if their global orientation remains more anxious or avoidant.
What is the relationship between attachment theory and therapy?
Attachment theory has influenced psychotherapy significantly, generating several attachment-informed therapeutic approaches. Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT), developed by Sue Johnson, is explicitly built on attachment theory and is one of the most empirically supported couples therapy approaches, with multiple RCTs demonstrating its effectiveness for relationship distress. The key therapeutic mechanism in EFT is helping partners identify the attachment needs and fears underlying their conflict — the anxious partner's pursuit is reframed as a desperate bid for connection; the avoidant partner's withdrawal is reframed as protection from the pain of anticipated rejection. When both partners can see this, the cycle that maintains their distress often softens. Individual therapies including EMDR, schema therapy, and mentalisation-based treatment (MBT) also draw on attachment principles.
