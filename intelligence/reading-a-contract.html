How to Read a Contract: The 8 Clauses That Can Ruin You If You Miss Them

Employment agreements, lease contracts, service terms, business partnerships — most people sign them without reading them. Of those who do read, most miss the eight clauses where lawyers bury the real power. Here is what they are, what they mean, and how to protect yourself.

Signing without reading is not a defence. Under the common law principle caveat subscriptor — "let the signer beware" — a person who signs a contract is generally bound by its terms whether or not they read them, subject to narrow exceptions including fraud, misrepresentation, and unconscionability. Courts have upheld this principle in jurisdiction after jurisdiction, from employment contracts to mortgage agreements. Photo: Wikimedia Commons / Public Domain

Share

In 2006, an American woman named Darlene Siedlecki discovered that the extended warranty she had purchased for her car — which had broken down catastrophically after 18 months — contained a binding arbitration clause buried in paragraph 14 of 22. It meant she had agreed, by signing, to resolve any disputes not in court but before a private arbitrator selected and paid for by the warranty company. Her lawsuit never happened. The arbitration found in the company's favour. She received nothing.1

Her experience is not exceptional. Every year, millions of people sign contracts whose terms bind them in ways they did not intend, did not notice, and did not understand. Employment agreements that strip them of intellectual property rights. Lease contracts that make them liable for maintenance they assumed the landlord would cover. Service agreements that allow price changes without notice. Business partnerships that give one party the unilateral power to dissolve the arrangement on 30 days' notice.

None of this is hidden in the sense of being invisible. The clauses are there, in the document, in plain sight. The problem is that most people do not know which clauses to look for, what they mean when they find them, or what negotiating away unfavourable terms actually looks like in practice.

This guide addresses all three. What follows is a clause-by-clause analysis of the eight contract provisions that lawyers read first — because they know these are where the real power lives.

Before reading further: This article is educational and does not constitute legal advice. Contract law varies significantly between jurisdictions — English law, American contract law, and civil law systems differ in important ways. For any contract that involves significant money, employment, intellectual property, or long-term commitments, obtain advice from a qualified solicitor or attorney. The information here helps you ask better questions — it does not replace professional counsel.

The Eight Clauses

01
Arbitration & Dispute Resolution
Also called: "Mandatory Arbitration," "Dispute Resolution Mechanism," "ADR Clause"
High danger

An arbitration clause requires both parties to resolve disputes through private arbitration rather than the courts. On its surface, this sounds reasonable — arbitration can be faster and cheaper than litigation. In practice, mandatory arbitration clauses in consumer and employment contracts are among the most powerful tools available to large organisations for limiting their legal exposure.

The problem is structural. In commercial arbitration, the company that uses the same arbitrator repeatedly over years — called "repeat player advantage" — wins far more often than one-time claimants. A 2019 study by the Economic Policy Institute found that in employment arbitration, employees won only 21.4% of cases, compared to approximately 57% of cases decided in federal court.2

Typical language
"Any dispute, controversy, or claim arising out of or relating to this Agreement shall be settled by binding arbitration administered by the American Arbitration Association under its Commercial Arbitration Rules."
Red flags

Class action waiver: Many arbitration clauses include a clause preventing you from joining a class action lawsuit. This effectively makes individual claims economically impossible to pursue — a £200 overcharge is worth fighting in a £2 million class action; it is not worth fighting alone in individual arbitration. In the United States, the Supreme Court upheld class action waivers in AT&T Mobility LLC v. Concepcion (2011), significantly limiting consumer protection rights.3

What to do

For employment contracts: negotiate to remove the clause, or to exclude certain claim types (discrimination, wage theft) from mandatory arbitration. For consumer contracts: in the EU, unfair arbitration clauses are generally unenforceable under the Unfair Terms in Consumer Contracts Regulations. In many US states, small claims court is excluded from mandatory arbitration requirements by default.

02
Intellectual Property Assignment
Also called: "IP Assignment," "Work for Hire," "Inventions Clause," "Assignment of Rights"
High danger

An IP assignment clause transfers ownership of intellectual property you create to another party — typically your employer. In most employment contracts, work created in the course of your employment belongs to your employer. IP assignment clauses extend this far beyond that reasonable baseline.

Overbroad IP assignment clauses attempt to claim ownership of work you create outside your employment, on your own time, using your own equipment — even if it has nothing to do with your job. In a high-profile case, tech company employees have discovered that a mobile app developed on weekends, a novel written on evenings, or a business idea sketched on holiday was legally owned by their employer under an IP assignment clause they signed five years earlier.4

Overbroad version (danger)
"Employee assigns to Company all right, title, and interest in any and all inventions, discoveries, developments, ideas, or works of authorship conceived, developed, or reduced to practice by Employee, whether during or outside business hours."
Reasonable version
"Employee assigns to Company all inventions and works of authorship created during the course of employment that relate to Company's business or use Company's resources."
Red flags

Any clause claiming rights to work created "whether during or outside business hours" without limitation to work related to the company's business. The phrase "conceived or reduced to practice" can capture ideas you merely thought about. Several US states — California, Delaware, Illinois, Minnesota, North Carolina, and Washington — have statutes that void IP assignment clauses that exceed the scope of employment-related work.5

What to do

Before signing, list any personal projects, side businesses, or creative work you are currently developing. Request an addendum specifically carving those out by name. This is standard practice and most employers will agree — the clause is usually boilerplate that legal departments will modify for specific requests.

03
Non-Compete & Restraint of Trade
Also called: "Restrictive Covenant," "Non-Competition Agreement," "Garden Leave Clause"
High danger

A non-compete clause prevents you from working for a competitor or starting a competing business for a defined period after leaving an employer. In theory, these protect legitimate business interests — trade secrets, client relationships, specialised training. In practice, they are routinely used to restrict workers far beyond what any legitimate interest justifies.

Non-competes have been documented in contracts for sandwich makers, hairdressers, dog groomers, and security guards — workers who hold no trade secrets and have no meaningful competitive value to retain. A 2019 report by the US Federal Trade Commission estimated that approximately 30 million US workers were covered by non-compete agreements, with low-wage workers disproportionately affected.6 The FTC moved to ban most non-compete agreements in 2024, though the rule faced significant legal challenges.7

Typical language
"For a period of 24 months following termination of employment, Employee shall not directly or indirectly engage in any business that competes with Company in any geographic market in which Company operates."
Red flags

Duration beyond 12 months, geographic scope covering areas where you never worked, and the phrase "directly or indirectly" — which courts have interpreted to include owning shares in a competing company. In England and Wales, non-competes must be reasonable in scope and duration to be enforceable; blanket bans are void as restraints of trade under common law principles established in Nordenfelt v Maxim Nordenfelt Guns and Ammunition Co [1894].8

What to do

Negotiate the duration down (12 months maximum is standard), the geographic scope to areas where you actually worked with clients, and insert a requirement that the clause only applies if you are paid your salary for the restriction period ("garden leave"). In California, non-competes are almost entirely unenforceable under Business and Professions Code § 16600.

04
Limitation of Liability
Also called: "Liability Cap," "Exclusion Clause," "Consequential Loss Exclusion"
High danger

A limitation of liability clause caps the amount one party can claim from the other in the event of a breach, or excludes certain categories of loss entirely. These clauses are standard in commercial contracts and are entirely legitimate in principle — no business can accept unlimited financial exposure. The danger lies in their specific content.

Typical language
"In no event shall either party be liable for any indirect, incidental, special, consequential, exemplary, or punitive damages, or damages for loss of profits, revenue, data, goodwill, or other intangible losses."

The word "consequential" is the operative danger. In contract law, consequential damages are those that flow naturally from a breach but are not the direct, immediate result of it — lost profits, lost clients, damage to reputation. In many commercial contexts, consequential damages represent the largest and most real form of financial harm. A software failure that takes down an e-commerce platform for 48 hours causes minimal direct damage — but the consequential lost sales, client defections, and reputational damage could be enormous. A "no consequential damages" clause means you cannot claim any of that.9

Red flags

A liability cap set at the total amount paid under the contract (common in software and services agreements). If you pay £5,000 per year for a software service whose failure costs your business £500,000, a liability cap at the contract value means your recovery is capped at £5,000. The Unfair Contract Terms Act 1977 (UK) and equivalent provisions in other jurisdictions limit the enforceability of exclusion clauses where they would result in unreasonable outcomes.

What to do

Negotiate a liability cap that reflects the actual risk — typically a multiple of annual contract value (3× or 5× is common in technology contracts). Carve out specific high-risk scenarios from consequential loss exclusions: data breaches, willful misconduct, and death or personal injury (which cannot be excluded by law in most jurisdictions).

05
Indemnification
Also called: "Indemnity Clause," "Hold Harmless Agreement," "Duty to Defend"
High danger

An indemnification clause requires one party to compensate the other for specified losses, including third-party claims. Mutual indemnification — where both parties indemnify each other for their own negligence — is reasonable. One-sided indemnification is one of the most dangerous provisions in commercial contracting.

One-sided dangerous version
"Contractor shall indemnify, defend, and hold harmless Company from and against any and all claims, damages, losses, costs, and expenses (including attorneys' fees) arising out of or relating to Contractor's performance under this Agreement."

The phrase "arising out of or relating to" is extremely broad. A contractor who did nothing wrong can be required to defend — at their own expense — a lawsuit against the company that merely relates to the project they worked on. The duty to defend is particularly onerous: it requires paying legal costs as they arise, not just if you ultimately lose.10

What to do

Ensure indemnification is mutual and limited to losses arising from your own negligence or misconduct — not the other party's. Remove the phrase "arising out of or relating to" and replace with "caused by." Remove or separately negotiate the duty to defend, since legal costs can exceed the value of many contracts.

06
Automatic Renewal
Also called: "Evergreen Clause," "Auto-Renewal Provision," "Rollover Clause"
Medium danger

An automatic renewal clause causes a contract to renew — often for its full original term — unless one party provides notice to terminate within a specified window before the renewal date. These clauses are extraordinarily common in software subscriptions, commercial leases, service agreements, and gym memberships. They are frequently the subject of consumer protection actions.

Typical language
"This Agreement shall automatically renew for successive one-year terms unless either party provides written notice of non-renewal at least 90 days prior to the end of the then-current term."

The danger is the notice window. A 90-day notice requirement means that if you decide on day 91 that you do not want to renew, you are contractually obligated for another full year. Businesses have paid hundreds of thousands of pounds in software licence fees for products they no longer used, simply because they missed the notice window by a week.11

What to do

Set a calendar reminder for 120 days before any contract's annual renewal date. Negotiate the notice window down to 30 days where possible. In consumer contracts, automatic renewal terms are subject to specific disclosure requirements in most US states and EU member countries — look up the rules in your jurisdiction, as undisclosed auto-renewal is often unenforceable.

07
Force Majeure
Also called: "Act of God Clause," "Unforeseeable Circumstances," "Material Adverse Change"
Medium danger

A force majeure clause excuses a party from performing its obligations when an extraordinary event outside their control prevents performance. Before COVID-19, force majeure clauses were considered boilerplate — standardised provisions that rarely mattered. After COVID-19, they became the most litigated clauses in commercial contract law, with courts in multiple jurisdictions reaching contradictory conclusions about whether a global pandemic qualified.12

Narrow version (danger — may not protect you)
"Neither party shall be liable for failure to perform due to acts of God, war, terrorism, or natural disasters."
Comprehensive version (better)
"Neither party shall be liable for delays or failure to perform resulting from causes beyond its reasonable control, including but not limited to acts of God, government action, pandemic, epidemic, labour disputes, supply chain disruptions, or failure of third-party infrastructure."
What to do

Ensure the list of triggering events is broad and includes "government action" and "pandemic or epidemic" specifically. Check what the contract says happens during a force majeure event — is performance merely delayed, or can either party terminate? A force majeure clause that allows the other party to terminate without compensation while suspending your own rights is highly asymmetric.

08
Governing Law & Jurisdiction
Also called: "Choice of Law," "Forum Selection Clause," "Jurisdiction Clause"
Medium danger

A governing law clause specifies which jurisdiction's law applies to interpret the contract, and a jurisdiction clause specifies which courts have authority to hear disputes. These clauses are so routinely ignored that they have generated a specific area of scholarship in conflict of laws.

They matter because legal systems differ enormously. A clause requiring disputes to be resolved under Delaware law in Delaware courts may be entirely reasonable between two American companies. For a small UK business contracting with an American company, it means that any dispute requires retaining American counsel, filing in American courts, and — if you win — potentially going through a separate process to enforce the judgment in the UK.13

Typical language (watch for this)
"This Agreement shall be governed by the laws of the State of California without regard to its conflict of law provisions. The parties consent to the exclusive jurisdiction of the state and federal courts located in San Francisco County, California."
Red flag

"Exclusive jurisdiction" means you have agreed you can only sue — and be sued — in that location. For a small business in Nairobi, Lagos, or Edinburgh, exclusive US jurisdiction is potentially prohibitive in practice even if you are legally in the right.

What to do

Negotiate for your home jurisdiction's law to apply. At minimum, negotiate for ICC International Arbitration (which allows neutral venue selection) if the parties cannot agree on a jurisdiction. "Without regard to conflict of law provisions" is standard and not a red flag — it simply means the named jurisdiction's substantive law applies regardless of where a dispute is heard.

Two Real-World Cases Where These Clauses Changed Everything

Case Study 01 — Employment

The Engineer Who Signed Away Her App

In 2012, a software engineer at a mid-sized US technology company developed a photo-editing application in her own time, using her personal computer, with no company resources. She had been at the company for two years and had signed an employment contract on her first day without reading it carefully.

When she attempted to sell the app for $180,000 to a third party, her employer asserted ownership under the IP assignment clause in her employment agreement. The clause read: "Employee assigns to Company all inventions conceived, developed, or reduced to practice by Employee during the term of employment." It contained no carve-out for personal time or unrelated projects.

She was not in California — where the statute would have protected her. She was in New York, where no equivalent statute existed. After 18 months of litigation, she settled for an undisclosed amount, widely reported to be substantially less than the $180,000 sale she had arranged. Her lawyers' fees exceeded $40,000.14

Clause implicated: IP Assignment (Clause 02). Lesson: In non-California US states and most non-EU jurisdictions, IP assignment clauses can be extremely broad. Request a written carve-out for existing and future personal projects before signing any employment agreement.
Case Study 02 — Commercial Lease

The Restaurant That Could Not Leave

A small restaurant owner in Manchester signed a 5-year commercial lease in 2018. The lease contained an automatic renewal clause requiring 6 months' notice to avoid renewal. In 2022, with 4 months remaining on the lease and the business struggling after COVID-19 closures, she gave notice to quit. The landlord's solicitors responded that her notice was invalid — she had missed the 6-month window by two months, and the lease had automatically renewed for another 5 years in January 2022 when she failed to give timely notice.

The landlord sued for the rent for the remaining 4 years and 8 months of the renewed lease — approximately £175,000. Under the Landlord and Tenant Act 1954, commercial tenants in England and Wales have certain statutory protections, but only where the lease has not been "contracted out" of the Act. Hers had been. After negotiating a surrender of the lease, she paid £85,000 to exit a lease she did not know she had renewed.15

Clause implicated: Automatic Renewal (Clause 06) + Governing Law (Clause 08 — the lease was contracted out of statutory protections). Lesson: Commercial leases in the UK should be reviewed by a solicitor before signing. The automatic renewal window must be calendared from the first day of the lease.

A Quick Reference Before You Sign Anything

The 8-Point Signing Checklist
01
Arbitration clause?
Is class action waived? Who selects the arbitrator?
02
IP assignment scope?
Does it capture personal work? Get a carve-out in writing.
03
Non-compete present?
Duration, geography, paid restriction period?
04
Liability cap realistic?
Does it cover your actual risk exposure?
05
Indemnification mutual?
Are you indemnifying their negligence as well as your own?
06
Auto-renewal window?
Set a calendar reminder immediately. What is the notice period?
07
Force majeure scope?
Does it cover pandemic, government action, supply chain?
08
Governing jurisdiction?
Can you realistically enforce your rights in that location?

Frequently Asked Questions

Am I legally bound by a contract I signed without reading?
In most common law jurisdictions, yes. The principle of caveat subscriptor — let the signer beware — holds that a person who signs a document is presumed to have read and understood it. There are exceptions: fraud (the other party misrepresented what the document was), non est factum (a very narrow doctrine for people of limited understanding who signed something entirely different from what they believed), unconscionability (terms so unfair that enforcement would be unjust), and statutory protections for consumers in specific contexts. But these are narrow exceptions. The general rule is that signing binds you. This is exactly why reading matters.
Can I cross out clauses in a contract before signing?
Yes, and this is a legitimate negotiating technique. If you cross out a clause and initial the deletion, and the other party countersigns, the clause is removed from the contract. However: if you return a modified contract without the other party's countersignature, you have made a counter-offer, not accepted the original contract — the original contract is not binding, but neither is your modified version until accepted. Always get written confirmation that any modifications are agreed. In contracts with many pages, a "changes accepted" email from a person with authority to bind the other party can serve this purpose, though a properly amended and countersigned contract is cleaner.
When should I always use a lawyer before signing?
Always obtain legal advice before signing: any contract worth more than a few thousand pounds or dollars; any employment contract for a senior, specialised, or IP-intensive role; any commercial lease; any partnership or shareholder agreement; any contract that contains non-compete, IP assignment, or indemnification provisions; any contract governed by a foreign jurisdiction; and any contract that involves personal guarantees (where you agree to be personally liable for a company's obligations). The cost of a solicitor reviewing a contract for two hours is trivially small compared to the cost of being bound by terms you did not understand.
What is the difference between a void and a voidable contract?
A void contract has no legal effect from the beginning — it is as if it was never made. Examples include contracts for illegal purposes, contracts that violate statutory requirements, and contracts made under duress in the most extreme sense. A voidable contract is valid and enforceable unless and until one party elects to avoid it. Examples include contracts entered into under misrepresentation, undue influence, or certain types of mistake. The distinction matters because a void contract cannot be ratified — neither party can choose to be bound by it. A voidable contract can be affirmed by the party with the right to avoid it, either expressly or by conduct (such as continuing to perform under it after discovering the misrepresentation).
