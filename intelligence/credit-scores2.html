How Credit Scores Work and Exactly How to Improve Yours in 6 Months

A credit score is not a judgement of your character. It is an algorithm — a specific mathematical calculation using five weighted factors. Once you know exactly what the algorithm measures and how each input is weighted, improving your score becomes a straightforward engineering problem rather than a mystery.

In October 2023, the US Federal Housing Finance Agency published data showing that a borrower with a FICO score of 760 or above taking out a $400,000 30-year fixed mortgage would pay approximately 6.5% interest. The same borrower with a score of 620 would pay approximately 8.1%. The difference is 1.6 percentage points — which sounds small until you calculate it over 30 years: the low-score borrower pays approximately $141,000 more in interest over the life of the loan.1

That $141,000 gap — greater than the average American's annual income — is generated by a three-digit number that most people do not fully understand and that almost nobody is ever formally taught about. Your credit score affects your mortgage, your car loan, your apartment rental application, your insurance premiums in many states, and in some industries, your job application. Understanding how it is calculated is not a minor financial literacy detail. It is one of the highest-return pieces of knowledge available to any adult in a credit economy.

What Credit Scores Actually Are

The term "credit score" is used loosely to refer to several different scoring models. In the United States, the dominant model is the FICO score, developed by the Fair Isaac Corporation and used by approximately 90% of top lenders.2 In the UK, the three main consumer credit reference agencies — Experian, Equifax, and TransUnion — each calculate their own proprietary scores on different scales. In the EU, credit scoring practices vary by country and are regulated differently from US practice.

This guide focuses primarily on the FICO model because it is the most widely used, most extensively documented, and most directly consequential for borrowing decisions — but the underlying principles apply across scoring systems, because all of them are fundamentally measuring the same thing: the statistical likelihood that you will repay a debt as agreed.

The Five Factors: Exactly How Your Score Is Calculated

FICO publishes the five factors and their approximate weightings that determine your score. This transparency is unusual — most proprietary algorithms are kept secret — and it means that improving your FICO score is genuinely knowable. The five factors, in order of weight:3

35%
Payment History
The single most important factor
Whether you have paid past credit accounts on time. A single 30-day late payment can drop a score in the 780 range by 90–110 points. A 90-day late payment can drop it by 110–130 points. Bankruptcies, foreclosures, and accounts sent to collections are the most damaging events — and remain on your credit report for 7 years (10 years for Chapter 7 bankruptcy in the US). Implication: nothing you do for the other four factors matters as much as paying every single bill on time, every month, without exception.
30%
Credit Utilisation
How much of your available credit you are using
Your credit utilisation ratio is the percentage of your total available revolving credit (credit cards, lines of credit) that you are currently using. If you have £10,000 in total credit limits and £3,000 in balances, your utilisation is 30%. FICO considers both total utilisation and per-card utilisation. The widely cited recommendation is to stay below 30% utilisation. Research by FICO itself shows that people with scores above 800 typically use less than 7% of their available credit.4 Importantly, utilisation is recalculated every month based on the balance reported by your lenders — which means it is the fastest factor to change in either direction.
15%
Length of Credit History
Age of your oldest account, newest account, and average age
FICO considers the age of your oldest credit account, your newest account, and the average age of all accounts. Longer history is better. This is why closing old credit cards — even ones you no longer use — can harm your score: it removes old accounts from the average age calculation and reduces your total available credit (increasing utilisation). Keep your oldest credit card open, even if you rarely use it.
10%
Credit Mix
Variety of credit types in your profile
FICO rewards having experience with different types of credit: revolving credit (credit cards), instalment loans (mortgages, car loans, student loans), and retail accounts. A person who has successfully managed multiple types of credit is statistically a better borrower than someone with only credit cards. Do not open new accounts purely to improve credit mix — the benefit is small and new accounts temporarily lower your score.
10%
New Credit Inquiries
Recent applications for new credit
When you apply for credit, the lender makes a "hard inquiry" on your credit report. Each hard inquiry typically reduces your score by 5–10 points and remains on your report for 2 years (though its score impact fades after 12 months). Multiple mortgage or auto loan inquiries within a 45-day window are treated as a single inquiry by FICO — rate shopping is not penalised. Soft inquiries — checking your own credit, employer background checks, pre-approval checks — do not affect your score.5

The 6-Month Improvement Plan: Month by Month

Given what we know about how the algorithm works, a structured 6-month plan can produce measurable improvements for most people. The exact point gain depends on your starting score and credit profile — someone at 580 has more room to improve quickly than someone at 740 — but the actions are the same.

1
Month 1 — Audit and eliminate errors
Potential impact: 20–100 points if errors found
Pull your full credit reports from all three bureaus. In the US, use AnnualCreditReport.com (the only federally mandated free source).6 In the UK, use the free statutory reports from Experian, Equifax, and TransUnion.
Check for errors — accounts that are not yours (possible identity theft), late payments incorrectly recorded, accounts showing a balance after being paid in full, and duplicate accounts.
Dispute errors immediately. Under the Fair Credit Reporting Act (US), bureaus must investigate disputes within 30 days. Errors are more common than most people expect — a 2021 FTC study found that 26% of consumers had at least one material error on their credit reports.7
Set up autopay for every credit account, minimum payment at minimum. This ensures Month 2 onwards does not produce new late payments, which are the most damaging possible event.
2
Month 2 — Attack credit utilisation
Potential impact: 20–80 points depending on current utilisation
Calculate your current utilisation per card and overall. Total balances ÷ total limits = overall utilisation percentage.
Pay down the highest-utilisation card first — not necessarily the highest-balance card. A card at 90% utilisation is hurting your score more than a card at 30% even if the 90% card has a smaller balance.
Request credit limit increases on existing cards without applying for new ones. A higher limit on the same card lowers your utilisation ratio without a hard inquiry (most issuers offer soft-inquiry limit increases). Wait until after any major loan application — do not do this in the month before applying for a mortgage.
Time your payments. Credit cards report balances to bureaus on a specific date each month (usually the statement closing date). Pay your balance down before that date — not just before the due date — to show a lower utilisation when reported.
3–4
Months 3–4 — Protect history, manage inquiries
Potential impact: 5–30 points; primarily prevents further drops
Do not close old accounts — especially your oldest credit card. If you are tempted by a new card offer, keep the old one open. Use it for a small recurring purchase (a streaming subscription) and autopay it in full.
Do not apply for new credit unless essential. Each hard inquiry costs 5–10 points. If you are planning to apply for a mortgage or car loan within 6 months, freeze new credit applications entirely.
Become an authorised user on a family member's old, well-managed credit card. If the primary cardholder has a long history and low utilisation, being added as an authorised user can boost your average account age and available credit. You do not need to use the card.
5–6
Months 5–6 — Consolidate gains and measure
Review, verify improvement, prepare for any upcoming credit application
Pull your credit reports again and verify disputes from Month 1 have been resolved. Confirm errors are removed.
Check your score through a free monitoring service (Credit Karma, Experian's free tier, or your bank's free score feature). Compare to your baseline from Month 1.
If applying for a mortgage or major loan, do so in Month 6 or later after all the above steps. Lenders typically pull scores from all three bureaus and use the middle score. A 20-point improvement can make a material difference to your interest rate.
Maintain the habits: autopay on every account, utilisation below 30% (ideally below 10%), no unnecessary hard inquiries, oldest accounts kept open. Credit score improvement is permanent if the habits are permanent.

Case Studies: Real Credit Score Transformations

Case Study 01 — Error Dispute

A Fraudulent Account and 112-Point Recovery

A 34-year-old teacher in Atlanta discovered during a mortgage pre-approval process that her credit score was 598 — far below the 680 minimum required by her lender. Pulling her full credit reports, she found an account with a major retailer showing $4,200 in delinquent balances that she had never opened. Identity theft had been used to open the account in her name three years earlier.

She filed disputes with all three credit bureaus, a police report for identity theft, and an identity theft affidavit with the FTC's IdentityTheft.gov portal. Under the Fair Credit Reporting Act, she was entitled to have the fraudulent account blocked from her credit reports within 4 days of providing the required documentation to the bureaus.10

Within 6 weeks, the fraudulent account was removed from all three bureau reports. Her score rose to 710. Her mortgage was approved at a rate 0.8 percentage points lower than she would have received at 598 — saving her approximately $67,000 over 30 years.

Key lesson: Credit report errors — including fraudulent accounts — are legally removable and can produce the largest and fastest score improvements available. Check all three bureau reports, not just one.
Case Study 02 — Utilisation Management

From 64% to 8% Utilisation: A 71-Point Improvement

A freelance designer in London had three credit cards with a combined limit of £12,000 and combined balances of £7,680 — 64% utilisation. Her Experian score was 612 (on Experian's 0–999 scale, equivalent to approximately Fair). She was paying minimum payments only.

Using a balance transfer card with a 0% introductory rate (requiring a one-time fee of 2.9%), she transferred her two highest-balance cards to the new card — a single hard inquiry — and continued paying the same monthly amount. Over 5 months, her total balances dropped to £6,400 on £16,000 total limits (40% utilisation) as the new card's limit increased her total available credit.

In month 6, a family member added her as an authorised user on a 12-year-old credit card with a £8,000 limit and 4% utilisation. Her average account age increased and her total available credit rose to £24,000 with balances at £6,400 — 27% utilisation. Her Experian score rose to 683 — a 71-point improvement in 6 months.

Key lesson: Reducing utilisation and strategically increasing available credit (through authorised user addition) are the two fastest-acting score improvement levers available.

What Does Not Work — Common Myths Debunked

Myth: Closing credit cards you no longer use improves your score. The opposite is true. Closing a card reduces your total available credit (increasing utilisation) and may lower your average account age. Keep old cards open — use them occasionally for a small recurring purchase to prevent the issuer closing them due to inactivity.

Myth: Paying off a collection account removes it from your report. In most cases, paying a collection account simply changes its status from "unpaid" to "paid collection" — it remains on your report for 7 years from the original delinquency date. In 2017, the three major bureaus implemented the National Consumer Assistance Plan, removing medical collections under £500 and paid medical collections — but non-medical paid collections generally remain. Negotiate "pay for delete" in writing before paying any collection agency, if possible.11

Myth: Checking your own credit score hurts it. Checking your own credit is a soft inquiry and has absolutely no effect on your score. Check it as often as you like — monthly monitoring is advisable for catching errors and tracking improvement.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does a late payment stay on your credit report?
In the United States, late payments remain on your credit report for 7 years from the date of the original delinquency under the Fair Credit Reporting Act. In the UK, most negative information remains for 6 years from the date of the default. However, the impact of a late payment on your score diminishes significantly over time — a 30-day late payment from 5 years ago has far less impact than one from 6 months ago. The most important thing is to make all current payments on time: new on-time payment history progressively outweighs older negative marks.
Can I improve my credit score if I have no credit history at all?
Yes, though it requires specific strategies since you cannot improve a history that does not exist — you have to build one from scratch. The most effective approaches are: (1) become an authorised user on a family member's well-managed, long-standing credit card — their history gets partially reflected in your report; (2) open a secured credit card, where you deposit cash as collateral for the limit, use it for small purchases, and pay it off monthly — most issuers graduate secured cards to unsecured after 12–18 months of good behaviour; (3) apply for a credit-builder loan from a credit union, where the loan amount sits in a savings account until you have paid it off, building payment history. Avoid "credit repair" companies — there is nothing they can do legally that you cannot do yourself for free.
Does my income affect my credit score?
No — your income is not a factor in any major credit scoring model, including FICO. Lenders use income separately in their own underwriting process (to assess debt-to-income ratio and ability to repay), but the credit score itself is calculated entirely from your credit history. This means it is possible to have an excellent credit score with a modest income, and a poor credit score with a high income. It also means that credit scores can be a structural disadvantage for people with erratic income who may struggle to pay on time during low-income periods, regardless of their average annual earnings.
Is the credit score I see on Credit Karma the same one lenders use?
Not exactly, though it is a useful approximation. Credit Karma shows VantageScore 3.0, a competing scoring model developed by the three major bureaus. Most mortgage and auto lenders use FICO Score 8 or the older FICO Score 5/4/2 models. VantageScore and FICO scores generally move in the same direction — if one improves, the other usually does too — but they can differ by 20–40 points in either direction because they weight factors slightly differently. For a more accurate picture of what mortgage lenders will see, use myfico.com (paid) or check whether your bank or credit card issuer provides your actual FICO score for free — many do.
