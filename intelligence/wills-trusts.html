The Difference Between a Will and a Living Trust — and Which One You Actually Need

Most people assume a will is sufficient. Many estate planning attorneys assume a living trust is always better. Both are oversimplifications. The right answer depends on your specific situation — and understanding the differences clearly is the only way to make a decision you will not regret.

In 2019, the estate of Aretha Franklin — the Queen of Soul, worth an estimated $80 million — became the subject of a protracted legal dispute among her four sons. The reason was not family animosity. It was that Franklin had died without a will. Her estate was forced through the Michigan probate courts for years before assets could be distributed. Legal fees consumed resources that would otherwise have gone to her heirs. Private details of her estate became public court records. All of it was avoidable.1

Franklin's case is not unusual. Studies consistently find that the majority of adults — including wealthy adults — die without adequate estate planning documents. A 2023 survey by Caring.com found that only 34% of American adults had a will or trust in place.2 Among those who do have documents, many have made the wrong choice for their circumstances — either using a simple will when a trust would have served them better, or paying for an expensive trust when a will would have been entirely sufficient.

This guide explains what each instrument actually does, how they differ, what each costs, what each fails to do, and — most importantly — which one is right for different situations.

Important note: Estate planning law varies significantly between jurisdictions. This article focuses primarily on US law (with reference to UK equivalents) and is intended to inform, not to replace qualified legal counsel. The right estate plan for your specific situation requires professional advice from a solicitor or estate planning attorney in your jurisdiction.

What a Will Actually Does — and Does Not Do

A will — formally called a "last will and testament" — is a legal document that expresses your wishes for how your assets should be distributed after your death, who should be responsible for your minor children (your "guardian" designation), and who should manage the distribution process (your "executor" or "personal representative").

What a will does not do is immediately transfer your assets. A will must be submitted to and validated by a probate court before it has any legal effect. Probate is the judicial process through which a court supervises the administration of a deceased person's estate — verifying the will's validity, identifying debts, and overseeing the distribution of assets to beneficiaries.3

This is the central limitation of a will that most people do not understand when they create one. In the United States, probate can take anywhere from several months to several years, depending on the state, the size of the estate, and whether the will is contested. It is a public process — anyone can access probate court records. And it is not free. Probate fees vary by state but typically range from 3% to 7% of the estate's gross value in attorneys' fees, executor commissions, and court costs.4

What a Living Trust Actually Does

A revocable living trust (the most common type used in estate planning) is a legal entity you create during your lifetime that holds your assets. You are typically both the trustee (the person managing the trust) and the beneficiary (the person benefiting from it) during your lifetime — so you retain complete control. You designate a successor trustee who takes over management of the trust assets when you die or become incapacitated, and you name the beneficiaries who receive those assets.

Because the assets are already in the trust — not in your personal name — they do not go through probate when you die. The successor trustee can distribute assets to beneficiaries within days or weeks of your death, without court involvement, without public records, and without probate fees.5

This is the fundamental difference: a will operates through the court system after your death; a trust operates outside it.

Irrevocable trusts (a different category entirely) can have tax planning benefits, but they require giving up control of your assets.6

Which One You Actually Need: A Decision Framework

Choose based on your situation

A will is probably sufficient if:

  • Your estate is relatively simple — primarily bank accounts, retirement accounts (which pass by beneficiary designation anyway), and personal property
  • You are young with modest assets and expect your situation to change significantly
  • Your state has a simplified or expedited probate process for smaller estates
  • Your primary concern is designating a guardian for minor children (trusts cannot do this alone)
  • Cost is a significant constraint and professional legal advice is not accessible
  • You are comfortable with your estate becoming a public record

A living trust is worth the cost if:

  • You own real estate — especially in multiple states, which would require separate probate proceedings in each
  • Privacy is important — you do not want your assets, debts, and beneficiaries to become public record
  • You want your heirs to receive assets quickly, without court delays
  • You are concerned about incapacity — a trust gives your successor trustee authority immediately, without a court conservatorship proceeding
  • Your estate is large enough that probate fees (3–7%) would significantly exceed trust creation costs
  • You have a blended family, special needs beneficiaries, or complex family dynamics

"The decision between a will and a trust is not primarily about taxes. It is about whether you want a judge to supervise the distribution of your estate — and whether your heirs can afford to wait."

— Denis Clifford, Estate Planning Basics (10th ed., 2022), Nolo Press. p. 747

The "Unfunded Trust" Problem — The Most Common Mistake

The single most common estate planning error: Creating a living trust but failing to transfer ("fund") your assets into it. If your house, bank accounts, and investments remain titled in your personal name — not the trust's name — they will still go through probate when you die, defeating the entire purpose of the trust. Surveys of estate planning attorneys consistently cite the unfunded trust as the most prevalent and consequential error they see.8

Funding a trust requires actively retitling each asset. Your real estate deed must be changed to show the trust as owner. Your bank accounts must be retitled. Your investment accounts must be transferred to the trust. This process takes time and often requires working with your bank, your county recorder's office, and potentially your mortgage lender. It is not automatic when the trust document is signed — and many people who believe they have a trust-based estate plan actually have an unfunded trust that provides none of the promised benefits.

Case Studies: When Each Document Made the Difference

Case Study 01 — When a Will Was Enough

A Simple Estate, Handled Quickly and Cheaply

A 42-year-old teacher in Ohio with two children, a modest home, a 403(b) retirement account, and approximately $45,000 in savings created a will naming her sister as executor and designating her children's father as their guardian. Her retirement account already had her children listed as beneficiaries by designation — bypassing probate automatically. Her bank accounts were joint accounts with her sister — also bypassing probate. The only asset requiring probate was her home, valued at $190,000.

Ohio's probate process for straightforward estates is relatively streamlined. The total probate cost — court fees, attorney fees, and executor commission — came to approximately $8,500 and took seven months. Her sister received the home's proceeds and distributed them as directed by the will.

Would a living trust have been better? Marginally — it would have saved some of the probate cost and timeline. But the will served its purpose for an estate of this size and simplicity, and she created it for $400 using an estate planning attorney. The math did not justify a $2,000+ trust.

Key lesson: For simple estates where the primary assets already have beneficiary designations, a will may be entirely adequate. The calculation changes when real estate is the primary asset in a state with expensive or slow probate.
Case Study 02 — When a Trust Saved a Family

Multi-State Property, Blended Family, Difficult Probate

A 68-year-old retired engineer in California owned his primary residence (valued at $1.2 million), a vacation property in Nevada, and investment accounts totalling approximately $800,000. He had children from a first marriage and a second wife. He died with only a will.

His estate faced probate in two states simultaneously — California (for the primary residence) and Nevada (for the vacation property), each with separate proceedings, attorneys, and fees. California probate fees for an estate of this size, calculated on gross value under the statutory fee schedule (Probate Code §10810), came to approximately $46,400 in combined attorney and executor fees. Nevada added further costs. Total probate expenses across both states exceeded $60,000 and the proceedings took 22 months.

Tensions between his children from his first marriage and his second wife — which would have been managed privately by a successor trustee under a trust — became the subject of court proceedings, creating a permanent family rift. His attorney estimated that a properly funded living trust, which would have cost approximately $3,500 to create, would have saved the family over $56,000 in fees and avoided the court proceedings entirely.9

Key lesson: For estates involving real property in multiple states, family complexity, or high gross value, a living trust almost always justifies its upfront cost many times over.

What Both Documents Miss — Essential Companions

Whether you choose a will or a trust, both require additional documents to constitute a complete estate plan. Most estate planning attorneys recommend four core documents:10

1

Durable Power of Attorney

Authorises a designated person to manage your financial and legal affairs if you become incapacitated. Without this, your family may need to obtain a court conservatorship — an expensive, intrusive judicial process — to manage your finances while you are alive but unable to act for yourself. A living trust handles this for trust assets; a durable POA covers assets outside the trust and other financial matters.

2

Healthcare Power of Attorney / Healthcare Proxy

Designates someone to make medical decisions on your behalf if you cannot. Entirely separate from financial documents — a financial POA does not cover healthcare decisions. Without this, hospitals follow state default rules about who can make decisions, which may not reflect your wishes.

3

Advance Healthcare Directive (Living Will)

Specifies your wishes for end-of-life medical care — whether you want life-sustaining treatment continued in various circumstances, organ donation preferences, and similar decisions. Provides guidance to both your healthcare proxy and medical providers. Reduces the emotional burden on family members having to make these decisions under stress.

4

Beneficiary Designations (Review and Update)

Retirement accounts (401(k), IRA, pension), life insurance policies, and certain bank accounts pass directly to named beneficiaries regardless of what your will or trust says. A will that says "everything to my spouse" does not override a 401(k) that still names an ex-spouse as beneficiary. Reviewing and updating beneficiary designations on all financial accounts is as important as creating the will or trust itself.

UK Equivalents and Key Differences

In England, Wales, Scotland, and Northern Ireland, the equivalent of a US will is simply a "will" — but the probate process (called "obtaining a grant of probate") works somewhat differently. England and Wales use a centralised Probate Registry system; there is no state-by-state variation as in the US. Probate fees are lower (a flat fee of £273 for estates over £5,000 as of 2024, plus any solicitor fees).11

The US-style revocable living trust has no precise UK equivalent. UK trusts exist primarily as irrevocable structures used for specific tax planning purposes and are subject to complex Inheritance Tax (IHT) rules that make them less suitable as general probate-avoidance tools. The UK's probate process, being somewhat simpler than the US system for most estates, also makes the trust-versus-will calculation less acute for most UK residents.12

The UK does have equivalent documents for healthcare and financial decision-making: the Lasting Power of Attorney (LPA), which comes in two forms — one for Property and Financial Affairs and one for Health and Welfare — is the UK equivalent of the combined financial and healthcare power of attorney.13

Frequently Asked Questions

If I have a living trust, do I still need a will?
Yes. Estate planning attorneys universally recommend pairing a living trust with a "pour-over will." This is a simple will that acts as a safety net — it directs any assets you forgot to transfer into the trust, or that came into your possession after the trust was created, to "pour over" into the trust at your death. The pour-over will must still go through probate, but it ensures no assets fall through the cracks. Critically, a will is also the only document that can designate a guardian for minor children — a living trust cannot do this. Anyone with a trust and minor children needs both documents.
Can I create a will or trust without a lawyer?
For a simple will, yes — online services such as Nolo, Trust & Will, and similar platforms offer legally valid will templates for £30–$200, and in many jurisdictions a handwritten (holographic) will is valid without any formal template. Whether you should create a will without legal advice depends on your situation's complexity. Simple estates with clear beneficiary intentions are reasonable DIY candidates. Any estate involving real property, minor children, potential creditor issues, significant assets, blended families, or cross-jurisdictional elements warrants professional legal advice — the cost of errors in estate planning is borne by your heirs, not you. A living trust is considerably more complex to both draft and fund correctly and is difficult to execute without professional assistance.
Does a living trust protect my assets from creditors?
A revocable living trust — the type discussed in this article — provides no creditor protection during your lifetime. Because you retain control and the ability to revoke the trust, courts treat its assets as your personal assets for creditor purposes. If you are sued, creditors can generally reach trust assets. Irrevocable trusts can provide creditor protection, but they require surrendering control over your assets and are subject to complex legal requirements including look-back periods for Medicaid planning. If creditor protection is your primary concern, consult a specialised asset protection attorney — the strategies go well beyond standard estate planning documents.
What happens if I die without a will or trust?
Dying without a valid will is called dying "intestate." Your estate will still go through probate, but instead of following your wishes, the court applies your state's or country's intestacy laws — a fixed legal formula that typically distributes assets to a spouse first, then children, then parents, then siblings. Intestacy laws cannot account for the nuances of your family situation: they do not recognise unmarried partners, stepchildren who were not legally adopted, close friends, or charitable wishes. A long-term partner who was not your legal spouse typically receives nothing under intestacy laws in most jurisdictions. The cost and timeline of intestate probate is generally similar to or longer than probate with a will, since the court must determine heirs rather than simply validating a document.
