What Causes Inflation — and How Governments Actually Fight It

Inflation is the most consequential economic force in daily life. It erodes savings, drives interest rates, determines whether ordinary people can afford housing and food, and has toppled governments. A complete, honest account of what causes it, the four main types, and what actually works to bring it down.

Cost of living crisis, 2022–23 — Global consumer price inflation peaked at multi-decade highs driven by post-pandemic demand, supply chain disruption, and energy price shocks following Russia's invasion of Ukraine.

Share

In October 2022, the United Kingdom's Office for National Statistics reported that consumer price inflation had reached 11.1% — the highest level in 41 years. Households that had budgeted carefully for years found themselves unable to cover grocery bills. Energy bills for some families had doubled or tripled in twelve months. The phrase "cost of living crisis" moved from economic jargon to the centrepiece of political debate. A prime minister resigned. The markets moved so fast that pension funds required a Bank of England bailout to avoid collapse.1

What had happened — and why? The honest answer requires understanding what inflation actually is, which forces create it, and why it is so difficult to control without causing significant collateral damage. Most popular explanations are either too simple or too ideological. The economic reality is more complex and more useful to understand.

What inflation means precisely: Inflation is a sustained, general rise in the price level of an economy — not a one-time price increase in a specific good, but a persistent increase across the basket of goods and services consumers buy. Measured by the Consumer Prices Index (CPI) in the UK, the CPI-U in the US, and the HICP in the Eurozone. A 5% annual rate means the same basket costs 5% more than twelve months ago.

The Four Types of Inflation

Type 1 — Demand-Pull Inflation
Most Common

Occurs when aggregate demand grows faster than the economy's capacity to produce. Too many people have too much money to spend, and there is not enough supply to meet demand, so sellers raise prices.

What causes it: Rapid economic growth, strong employment, fiscal stimulus, very low interest rates, or sudden windfalls like pandemic relief payments. The post-pandemic inflation of 2021–22 had a significant demand-pull component — the US CARES Act alone injected over $2 trillion into the economy while supply chains had not yet recovered.2

Classic indicator: Unemployment is low, wages are rising, consumer confidence is high, and prices rise across the board simultaneously.
Type 2 — Cost-Push Inflation
Supply-Side Driver

Occurs when production costs rise and businesses pass those costs to consumers. Particularly vicious because it raises prices while simultaneously reducing output and employment — creating stagflation.

What causes it: Energy price surges, supply chain disruption, currency depreciation, or geopolitical shocks. The 1973 OPEC oil embargo quadrupled global oil prices overnight. The 2022 European inflation spike was similarly cost-push — Russia's invasion caused natural gas prices to rise over 300%.3

Classic indicator: Inflation concentrated in energy, food, and transport first, then spreading to manufactured goods. Unemployment may be rising simultaneously.
Type 3 — Built-In (Wage-Price) Inflation
Self-Sustaining Spiral

Workers demand higher wages to compensate for rising prices, businesses raise prices to cover higher wage costs, which prompts further wage demands — a self-reinforcing loop that sustains inflation long after its original cause disappears.

Why it matters: Once inflation expectations become unanchored from the central bank's target, they are extremely difficult and painful to reverse — as the US and UK discovered in the early 1980s when it took severe recessions to break the inflation psychology of the 1970s.4

Classic indicator: Wages rising faster than productivity, aggressive union bargaining, surveys showing consumers expect high inflation to continue for years.
Type 4 — Monetary Inflation
Milton Friedman's Framework

Friedman's dictum: "Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon." If money supply grows faster than productive capacity, each unit of money buys less. Correct in extreme cases — Weimar Germany 1923, Zimbabwe 2008, Venezuela 2018 are all monetary phenomena. The relationship is weaker at normal inflation rates in modern economies.5

Classic indicator: Government financing spending by printing money. Exchange rates collapsing. Prices rising in triple or quadruple digits annually.

How Governments Actually Fight Inflation

The primary tool is monetary policy — raising interest rates to increase borrowing costs, reduce consumer spending, reduce demand, and reduce inflation. In practice considerably more complicated than the theory.

Two Case Studies

Case Study 01 — Paul Volcker and the 1980s Disinflation

How America Broke the Inflationary Spiral — At Enormous Cost

By 1979, US inflation had reached 13.3% and was accelerating. The wage-price spiral had become self-sustaining. Inflation expectations were deeply unanchored.

Paul Volcker raised the federal funds rate to 20% by June 1981. The result: a severe double-dip recession. Unemployment reached 10.8% in 1982 — the highest since the Great Depression. Volcker received death threats and protests outside the Federal Reserve building.6

It worked. By 1983 inflation had fallen to 3.2%. The lesson: central bank credibility, once lost, can only be restored through pain. The longer high inflation persists, the more costly the eventual cure.

Key lesson: Entrenched inflation requires dramatic action. The cost of inaction — permanently unanchored expectations — would have been even higher.
Case Study 02 — Zimbabwe's Hyperinflation (2007–2009)

What Happens When a Government Destroys Its Own Currency

At its peak in November 2008, Zimbabwe's annual inflation rate was estimated at 89.7 sextillion percent. The Reserve Bank issued a 100 trillion dollar note. Prices doubled roughly every 24 hours. The formal economy ceased to function.7

The cause was unambiguously monetary: the government financed spending by printing money rather than through taxation or borrowing. Each round of money printing caused further currency collapse, which required more printing — a perfect hyperinflationary spiral.

Zimbabwe abandoned its currency in 2009, adopting the US dollar. Inflation immediately stopped. Hyperinflation is always a political failure as much as an economic one.

Key lesson: Hyperinflation happens when institutions break down. The cure requires external anchoring to be credible.

What Inflation Means for You

Savings: Inflation erodes the real value of cash. If inflation runs at 5% and your savings account pays 2%, you are losing 3% purchasing power per year. Over ten years, the real value of £10,000 falls to approximately £7,400.8

Debt: Inflation helps debtors and hurts creditors. With a fixed-rate mortgage, the real value of your debt declines as prices rise — you repay in money worth less than when you borrowed.

Investments: Equities and property historically provide inflation protection because companies and landlords can raise prices. Fixed-coupon bonds are hurt — their payments become worth less in real terms.

"Inflation is taxation without legislation. It is the cruelest tax of all, because it falls most heavily on those who can least afford it."

— Milton Friedman, Free to Choose (1980)5

High inflation consistently redistributes wealth from savers to debtors, from fixed-income recipients to asset owners, from the poor to those wealthy enough to hold inflation-resistant assets. Understanding it is not merely academic — it is essential for protecting your financial position.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why do central banks target 2% inflation rather than 0%?
Zero inflation leaves no buffer against deflation — falling prices — which is more dangerous than moderate inflation. Deflation causes consumers to delay purchases, reducing demand, slowing the economy further. Japan's "lost decade" was largely a deflationary phenomenon. A 2% target also gives central banks room to cut real interest rates below zero when needed, and helps "grease the wheels" of labour markets by allowing real wages to adjust without nominal pay cuts that workers strongly resist.
Does raising interest rates always work to bring inflation down?
It works eventually but the mechanism is slow, uneven, and causes collateral damage. The transmission takes 12–24 months. If inflation is supply-side (oil shock, disruption), rate rises slow the economy without addressing the supply problem — they work by reducing demand enough to offset the constraint, meaning accepted output and employment losses. The 2022–24 rate rises did bring inflation back toward target but at the cost of slower growth, housing market downturns, and higher mortgage costs. Economists call this the "sacrifice ratio."
Is inflation always bad?
No. Moderate, stable, predictable inflation is associated with healthy economic conditions. The 2% central bank target reflects the consensus that low positive inflation is optimal. It helps debtors, encourages spending over hoarding, and lubricates labour markets. The problem is high, volatile, or unexpected inflation — which erodes purchasing power unpredictably and disproportionately hurts those on fixed incomes.
How does inflation affect Kenya and developing economies differently?
Currency depreciation — when the shilling weakens against the dollar — directly feeds into import prices since food, fuel, and manufactured goods are priced in dollars. A 10% currency depreciation can add 3–5 percentage points to consumer inflation within months. Central banks in developing economies face a harder trade-off: higher interest rates required to fight inflation, while also managing dollar-denominated government debt whose servicing costs rise when US rates go up.
