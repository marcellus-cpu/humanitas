How the Stock Market Actually Works — A Complete, Honest Explanation

What a stock actually is, how prices are really set, why markets go up and down, what drives the gap between news and prices, why most professional fund managers underperform, and what forty years of research says about the best long-term strategy for ordinary investors.

In the summer of 1720, Sir Isaac Newton — the man who had mathematically described the motion of the planets — lost the equivalent of several million pounds sterling in the South Sea Company stock collapse. Asked about it afterwards, he reportedly said that he could calculate the motions of heavenly bodies but not the madness of people. Newton had watched the stock rise, sold at a profit, watched it rise further, bought back in at the peak, and lost almost everything when the bubble collapsed.1

Three hundred years later, the basic dynamics Newton encountered — prices driven by psychology as much as fundamentals, markets capable of both remarkable efficiency and spectacular irrationality, professional and amateur investors alike making predictable errors — remain the central puzzles of financial economics. The stock market is the most studied, most discussed, and most misunderstood financial institution in the world.

This article is an attempt to explain what the stock market actually is, how it actually works, and what the best evidence says about how to participate in it — not as entertainment, not as speculation, but as a long-term wealth-building mechanism. The honest answer to the last question is both simpler and more counterintuitive than most financial media suggests.

Note on scope: This article covers equity markets (stocks and shares) primarily in the context of long-term investment for ordinary individuals. It does not cover options, derivatives, short-selling, day trading, or other instruments that require specialist knowledge and carry substantially higher risks. The information here is educational and does not constitute personalised financial advice.

What a Stock Actually Is

A stock (also called a share or equity) is a unit of ownership in a company. When a company sells stock, it is selling fractions of itself — each share represents a proportional ownership stake in the company's assets, earnings, and future profits. If a company has 1,000,000 shares outstanding and you own 10,000 of them, you own 1% of the company.

This ownership has two potential sources of financial return. First, dividends: if the company distributes a portion of its profits to shareholders, you receive 1% of that distribution. Second, capital appreciation: if the company becomes more valuable (because it earns more money, or because investors become more optimistic about its future earnings), the price of each share rises and your 1% stake is worth more.2

Why do companies sell shares? Because selling ownership stakes is one of the primary ways companies raise capital to invest in growth. The alternative is borrowing (debt financing). Selling equity does not require interest payments or repayment of principal, but it means diluting existing owners' stakes and ceding partial control. The balance between debt and equity financing is one of the central decisions in corporate finance.

How Stock Prices Are Actually Set

Stock prices are not set by a central authority — they emerge from the continuous interaction of buyers and sellers in a market. At any moment, there are people who want to buy shares (bidders) and people who want to sell them (askers). A trade happens when a buyer's maximum price meets or exceeds a seller's minimum price. The price of the last completed transaction is "the price" of the stock at that moment.

What drives the price higher or lower is the balance between buying and selling pressure — and that balance is driven by expectations about the company's future earnings. If investors collectively revise upward their expectations of a company's future profits, the stock price rises. If expectations fall, the price falls. Current information affects prices only to the extent that it changes expectations about the future. This is why prices can fall on "good news" (if the news was less good than expected) and rise on "bad news" (if the news was less bad than feared).

The fundamental insight: a stock price is not a record of past performance but a collective estimate of future discounted cash flows. Two investors can look at the same company and reach different prices because they have different expectations about the future — and both can be rational given their information.
2
Stock Exchanges and How Trading Actually Happens

A stock exchange is a marketplace — historically a physical place, now primarily electronic — where buyers and sellers of securities meet. The New York Stock Exchange (NYSE), NASDAQ, the London Stock Exchange (LSE), and similar institutions provide the infrastructure: the rules, the technology, the clearing and settlement systems that make it possible for millions of transactions to occur reliably every day.

Modern stock markets operate through electronic order books that continuously match buy and sell orders. When you place a "market order" to buy shares, you get the best currently available price. A "limit order" specifies the maximum price you are willing to pay, and the order executes only if a seller matches that price. The spread between the best available buy price (bid) and the best available sell price (ask) is called the bid-ask spread, and it represents a cost of trading that market makers — firms that commit to always buying or selling at quoted prices — capture as compensation for providing liquidity.

The vast majority of stock market transactions are now executed by algorithmic systems in microseconds. High-frequency trading firms use sophisticated algorithms to make tiny profits on enormous volumes of trades, contributing to market liquidity but also raising concerns about fairness and market stability.3

3
Market Indices — What the "Stock Market" Number Actually Measures

When a news broadcast says "the stock market rose 1.5% today," it is almost certainly referring to a specific index — a statistical composite of a selected group of stocks designed to represent broader market performance. The most commonly cited indices are the S&P 500 (the 500 largest US companies by market capitalisation), the Dow Jones Industrial Average (30 large US companies), the FTSE 100 (100 largest UK companies), and the MSCI World Index (approximately 1,500 companies across 23 developed countries).

Each index is constructed differently and measures something different. The S&P 500 is market-capitalisation weighted — the largest companies have the most influence on the index's movements. The Dow Jones is price-weighted — a company with a higher share price has more influence, regardless of its total size. For most purposes, the S&P 500 is a more representative measure of the US stock market; the Dow Jones is historically important but less analytically meaningful.4

An index is like a weather average for a city — it tells you something real and useful about the overall climate, but it smooths over enormous variation between individual companies, sectors, and investment styles.

The Three Things That Drive Markets: Earnings, Interest Rates, and Psychology

The stock market's movements can be explained by three fundamental forces, operating across different time horizons.

Earnings are the primary long-run driver. Over periods of decades, stock prices track corporate earnings — if companies collectively earn more, their stocks are worth more. The relationship between a company's current earnings and its share price is measured by the price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio. The long-run average P/E ratio for the S&P 500 is approximately 15–17, meaning investors have historically paid about 15–17 times a company's annual earnings to own a share of it. When P/E ratios rise significantly above historical averages, it typically means either that earnings are expected to grow rapidly or that the market is overvalued.5

Interest rates have a powerful inverse relationship with stock prices. When interest rates rise, several things happen: bonds and savings accounts become more attractive relative to stocks (since they now pay higher returns with less risk), the cost of corporate borrowing rises (reducing future earnings), and the discount rate used to value future earnings increases (which reduces the present value of those earnings). For all these reasons, rising interest rates tend to push stock prices down and falling rates tend to push them up. The Federal Reserve's interest rate decisions are among the most closely watched variables in financial markets.6

Psychology and sentiment dominate short-run movements. Keynes described the stock market as a "beauty contest" in which investors are not trying to identify the best companies but to predict what other investors will consider valuable — a second-order problem of expectations about expectations. Fear and greed cycle through markets with regularity; investors systematically overreact to both good and bad news, creating predictable patterns of overvaluation and undervaluation that skilled analysts try to exploit and that behavioural economists have documented extensively.7

The Efficient Market Hypothesis — The Most Important Idea in Investing

In 1970, the economist Eugene Fama published a paper that formalised a hypothesis that had been circulating in academic finance for years: that stock prices in well-functioning markets reflect all available information. If this is true, it has a devastating implication: you cannot consistently beat the market by analysing information that is already available, because that information is already incorporated into prices.8

Fama identified three forms of market efficiency. The weak form holds that prices reflect all past trading information — so technical analysis (analysing price charts) cannot generate consistent returns. The semi-strong form holds that prices reflect all publicly available information — so fundamental analysis (studying company financials, reading annual reports) also cannot consistently generate excess returns. The strong form holds that prices reflect even private information — which Fama himself noted is obviously false, since insider trading demonstrably generates excess returns before prohibition and enforcement.

The empirical evidence broadly supports weak and semi-strong efficiency in developed markets, with qualifications. It is very difficult to consistently outperform the market using publicly available information after adjusting for risk and transaction costs. This is why the vast majority of actively managed funds underperform their benchmark index over long time horizons.

Two Landmark Case Studies: Lessons That Changed Investing

Case Study 01 — John Bogle and the Index Fund Revolution

How One Man Changed What Millions of People Do With Their Money

In 1976, John Bogle launched the First Index Investment Trust — the first index fund available to retail investors. The fund was designed to hold every stock in the S&P 500 in proportion to its market capitalisation, charge minimal fees, and do no stock selection whatsoever. It was widely mocked by the financial industry as "Bogle's folly." Nobody in the investment management business wanted to believe that the entire structure of active management — fund managers, research analysts, stockpickers — was essentially unable to generate value for clients after fees.

Bogle's argument was mathematical and relentless: in aggregate, all investors own the entire market. Before costs, the average investor earns market returns. After costs, the average investor earns less than market returns. Therefore, the lower your costs, the closer to market returns you keep. Since market returns have historically been positive over long periods, a low-cost index fund must, on average, outperform the average actively managed fund over time.9

Forty-eight years later, index funds manage over $15 trillion globally. Vanguard — Bogle's firm — is one of the largest asset managers in the world. And the data has fully vindicated Bogle's argument: the evidence for active underperformance after fees across most asset classes and time horizons is now overwhelming. The SPIVA (S&P Indices Versus Active) scorecard, published semi-annually since 2002, has consistently shown that roughly 80–90% of active funds underperform their benchmark index over 15-year periods.10

Key lesson: The index fund revolution is the most significant improvement in investment outcomes for ordinary people in financial history. The mathematical case for low-cost passive investing is not a matter of ideology — it is arithmetic.
Case Study 02 — The Dot-Com Bubble (1995–2002) and What Markets Forget

How a Genuine Technological Revolution Produced Catastrophic Overvaluation

The late 1990s internet boom was, in retrospect, both a genuine technological revolution and one of the most spectacular market bubbles in history. Between 1995 and March 2000, the NASDAQ Composite index rose from approximately 750 to 5,049 — a 570% increase in five years. Companies with no revenue, no profits, and sometimes no coherent business model achieved valuations of hundreds of millions of dollars. The argument was that traditional valuation metrics (price-to-earnings ratios, discounted cash flow analysis) did not apply to the "new economy."

Between March 2000 and October 2002, the NASDAQ fell from 5,049 to 1,114 — a decline of 78%. Several trillion dollars of market capitalisation was destroyed. Companies including Pets.com, Webvan, and eToys — which had collectively been worth billions — ceased to exist. Many of the technologies involved (e-commerce, online communication, digital media) did go on to transform the economy — but investors who bought at peak prices in 2000 would not recover to those prices for 15 years.11

The dot-com bubble illustrates several enduring lessons. First: technological revolutions do not automatically produce investment returns — the question is whether current prices already reflect the revolution's benefits. Second: valuation matters even for exciting companies. Third: the argument that "this time is different" has a 100% failure rate in financial history. Fourth: recoveries from severe bear markets can take much longer than investors expect.

Key lesson: Market prices can disconnect from fundamental value for extended periods. Even when the long-run direction is correct (the internet did transform the economy), the price you pay determines your return. Overpaying for a correct idea can still produce catastrophic losses.

The comparison is not a close contest in most market segments. The fee differential alone — typically 1–1.5% per year more for active management — compounds dramatically over time. An investor who pays 1.5% more per year for 30 years retains approximately 36% less wealth at the end of that period from the fee drag alone, before any consideration of whether the active manager generated excess returns (which, on average, they do not).12

What This Means for an Ordinary Investor

The academic evidence converges on a set of practical principles that are remarkably consistent across decades of financial research. They are also remarkably simple — and because they are simple, they are routinely ignored in favour of more exciting narratives.

  • Start early and stay invested. The S&P 500 has returned approximately 10% per year on average over the last 100 years (about 7% after inflation). An investor who invested £10,000 at age 25 and left it untouched would have approximately £174,000 at 65 at that rate. An investor who waited until 35 would have approximately £67,000. Ten years of earlier investment more than doubles the outcome.13
  • Use low-cost index funds. The evidence for passive outperformance after fees is overwhelming. A global equity index ETF with a fee of 0.10–0.20% per year captures the returns of hundreds of companies across dozens of countries without requiring stock selection skill.
  • Diversify broadly. Individual company stocks carry "idiosyncratic risk" — the risk specific to that company that disappears when you hold many companies. A portfolio of 500 stocks has far lower volatility per unit of expected return than a portfolio of 5 stocks. Global diversification reduces exposure to any single country's economic performance.14
  • Ignore short-term movements. The market will fall 10% or more in most years and 20% or more in some years. Investors who sell during declines lock in losses and miss recoveries. The data shows that market timing — moving in and out of the market based on short-term predictions — destroys value for almost all investors almost all the time.15
  • Increase bonds as you approach your goal. Equities generate higher long-run returns but with higher short-term volatility. For a 30-year investment horizon, short-term volatility is irrelevant. For a 5-year horizon (approaching retirement, saving for a house), it matters enormously. Gradually shifting toward lower-volatility assets as your time horizon shortens is standard financial planning advice.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is investing in the stock market the same as gambling?
They share superficial similarities — both involve uncertainty and the possibility of loss — but they are structurally different. In gambling, the expected value of most bets is negative: the house has an edge, and over time, gamblers lose. In the stock market, the long-run expected return has historically been positive — approximately 7% per year after inflation for global equities over the last century — because you are investing in the productive capacity of real companies that generate real earnings. The uncertainty is real: you can lose money over short time periods, and specific companies can go to zero. But over long time horizons, the expected return is positive, and the risk is substantially reduced by diversification. Day trading individual stocks with short time horizons is closer to gambling in its expected outcomes; diversified long-term index fund investing is not.
Can you predict when the stock market will crash?
No — and the evidence is extremely clear on this. Researchers, including Nobel Prize winners, have spent decades trying to find reliable indicators of market crashes and have largely failed. Individual crashes look obvious in retrospect; before the fact, the signals are ambiguous and the timing is essentially unknowable. The more interesting finding, from decades of academic research, is that even imperfect crash prediction is not profitable for investors, because the costs of being out of the market during the recovery typically exceed the costs of staying invested through the decline. The S&P 500's best 10 days in any given decade account for a large fraction of its total returns — and those best days frequently occur during or just after market downturns. Missing them by trying to time the market costs more than the crashes themselves.
What is the difference between a stock market crash and a recession?
A stock market crash is a rapid large decline in equity prices — typically defined as a fall of 20% or more from a recent high (a "bear market"). A recession is a sustained decline in economic activity, typically defined as two consecutive quarters of negative GDP growth. The two are related but not identical. Stock markets are forward-looking and often decline before recessions begin (markets try to price in anticipated economic weakness). They also often recover before recessions end (markets begin pricing in recovery while the economy is still contracting). This is why "the stock market is not the economy" — the market reflects expectations about the future, not the current state of economic activity. Stock markets have also declined sharply in the absence of recessions, and recessions have occurred with only modest market declines.
How much money do you need to start investing?
In practical terms, very little — fractional share investing and low-minimum index fund platforms have reduced the barrier to entry to essentially zero. In the UK, a Stocks and Shares ISA can be opened with most major platforms with no minimum investment. In the US, most major brokerages including Vanguard, Fidelity, and Schwab offer index ETFs with no minimum purchase and no commissions. The more important threshold is not the amount invested but the consistency of investing: regular contributions — even small ones — compounded over decades are far more powerful than larger irregular ones. Starting with £50 per month at 25 is substantially better than starting with £500 per month at 45, despite the higher nominal contribution in the second case.
